# Your Unfair Advantage: How Your Weird Background Justifies Premium Positioning

I have a weird resume.

3 years in tech sales. 2 years in marketing. 1 year in product. 4 years running a startup. Psychology coursework. 6 months of blog writing nobody asked for.

By credential standards: Jack of all trades, master of none.

But here's what that weird combination gives me competitively:

- I speak engineer language (worked there)
- I understand marketing (did that work)
- I've made product decisions (worked there)
- I understand startup chaos (lived it)
- I understand behavior change (studied it)
- I understand content/positioning (wrote about it)

Someone with just engineering background can't do what I do.
Someone with just a marketing degree can't do it.
Someone with just an MBA-focused curriculum can't do it.

The weird combination is worth $5-10K more per project than a specialist.

And I spent 3 years apologizing for it.

## Why Credentials DON'T Justif Premium Pricing (But Your Weird Background Does)

Credentials signal: "I have the minimum qualification for this work."

Everyone recognizes them. Everyone respects them. And everyone can get them.

**The real competitive advantage isn't credentials. It's weird combinations that nobody else has.**

Example:

- Generic: "I'm a marketing consultant" ($150/hour)
- Credentialed: "I'm a marketing consultant with MBA from Stanford" ($175/hour)  
- Specific-weird: "I help tech founders sell like engineers who understand marketing — because I was one" ($300/hour, $10K projects)

The third one commands 2x the price because it's:
- Specific (tech founders, not "anyone")
- Weird (rare combination: engineer + sales thinking)
- Defensible (only a tiny percentage of people have this combo)

Someone with a Harvard MBA can't do this work. They lack the weird combination.

But you have it.

## How to Mine Your Weird Resume (The 3-Step Process)

Most people have unusual backgrounds. They've just learned to apologize for them.

### STEP 1: List Your Actual Experience (Not Credentials)

Don't think "degree" or "certification." Think actual work and weird experiences.

**My entry:**
- Sold tech products to enterprises (3 years)
- Built marketing from nothing at startup (2 years)
- Made product decisions (1 year)
- Ran a company into the ground (4 years)
- Studied psychology of persuasion (6 months before getting bored)
- Wrote about business for free (2 years)

Now, don't filter. List yours.

### STEP 2: Find the Intersection (The Weird Combination)

Look for combinations that are *rare*.

**What makes it rare:**
- It requires experience in 3+ different areas
- Most people specialize (so they miss the connection)
- The combination solves a specific problem nobody else grasps

**My unusual combination:**
- Sales (understanding buyer behavior) 
- + Marketing (understanding messaging)
- + Engineering thinking (product thinking is different from sales thinking)
- = I understand how to position products to engineers

This is rare. 1% of people have this.

**Another example** (a student of mine):
- Copywriting (experience)
- + Fundraising (ran startup)
- + Sales (closed deals at previous company) 
- = "I help founders write pitch decks that convert investors"

This is also rare. Copywriters don't understand fundraising. Fundraisers aren't copywriters. Salesman don't write.

But his weird combination solves a specific problem, so he charges $3K per pitch deck and has a waiting list.

### STEP 3: Find Your Ideal Customer (The One Person Who *Needs* Your Weird Combo)

Not everyone benefits from your weird background.

But someone does. Specifically.

**For me:**
- Tech companies (need someone who speaks engineer talk)
- That are growing (need good positioning)
- That aren't yet Series B (don't have massive marketing budgets yet)
= Perfect fit: $3-5K projects, value I provide is disproportionate

**For my copywriter friend:**
- Startup founders (have product)
- Raising Series A (need to raise money)
- Who haven't written pitch decks (need writing help)
= Perfect fit: $3K per deck, founders are desperate for this specific help

**For you:**
- Who is the person for whom your weird background is THE perfect fit?
- Not "everyone who can use my skill." Specific person.
- What problem do they have that ONLY someone with your weird combo can solve?

## The Specificity Formula

Once you've found your weird combination, you need to make it specific to a market.

Generic: "I help solopreneurs build better businesses"

This could be anyone. Competes on price and credibility alone.

Specific: "I help solopreneurs who are burned out from scaling too fast turn their business into a sustainable operation without sacrificing ambition"

This is narrower. It speaks to a specific problem. Weird background (burnout + scaling experience + psychology knowledge) is relevant.

The formula:

```
"I help [specific audience] with [specific problem] by bringing [your weird combination] to bear"
```

Example 1: "I help SaaS founders who are technical but struggle with marketing by bridging the gap between product thinking and customer messaging (I was an engineer who learned sales and marketing the hard way)"

Example 2: "I help coaches and consultants who are great at results but terrible at systems so you can scale without reinventing (I'm a systems thinker who worked with creative people and realized they think differently about process)"

Example 3: "I help executives transitioning to entrepreneurship by preparing them for the reality of uncertainty without losing the discipline they've built (I made that transition; I understand both worlds)"

Each one:
- Names a specific audience
- Names a specific problem
- Explains why your weird background matters
