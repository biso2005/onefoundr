# Raise Your Prices: The Exact Framework That Won't Lose Clients

Most solopreneurs don't raise prices. They stay at the same rate for 3-5 years.

Meanwhile:
- Inflation is 3-5% annually (you're getting paid less every year)
- Their expertise improved 10x (but price is same)
- Market rates went up 30-40% (they have no idea)
- They're still at $100/hour when the market is $200/hour

This is costing them money every year.

Raised my rates from $150 → $250/hour (67% increase) in one year.

Predicted outcome (based on fear): lose 30% of clients, net 10% revenue increase
Actual outcome: lost 0 clients from my existing contracts, gained new clients at $250/hour

The math: Same revenue with 30% fewer hours (and better clients).

---

## Why You Should Raise Prices (The Real Reasons)

### Reason 1: Your costs go up

- Inflation: 3-5% annually
- Software: +5-10% per year
- Your time: You're busier, have more demands

If you don't raise prices, you're cutting your own salary to keep clients happy.

**Real math:**
- Year 1: $100/hour feels good
- Year 5 (with no increases): $100/hour = $77 in Year 1 dollars (you got a 23% pay cut)

### Reason 2: You're worth more than before

When you started, you had:
- 0 case studies
- 0 testimonials
- 0 proven results

Now you have:
- 20+ projects completed
- Results you can point to
- Clients who rave about you

This is worth more. Charge for it.

### Reason 3: Better-qualified clients

Here's the counterintuitive principle: **Higher prices attract better clients.**

- Price = quality signal. High price suggests quality.
- Low price attracts bargain hunters who negotiate everything
- Higher price attracts serious, committed clients

**Real client quality difference:**

| Hourly Rate | Client Profile | Friction Level | Revenue Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| $50/hr | Desperate/DIY crowd | Very high (negotiate constantly) | Volatile (client might disappear) |
| $100/hr | Small business owners | Medium (normal negotiation) | Moderate (steady but demanding) |
| $200/hr | Growing founders | Low (understand value) | High (committed to results) |
| $300+/hr | Scaling companies | Very low (know what they want) | Very high (pay on time, refer others) |

I went from $150 → $250/hour. Client quality went UP, meaning:
- Less negotiation on scope
- Fewer scope creep requests
- Faster payment
- More referrals
- Higher satisfaction (they feel they got their money's worth)

### Reason 4: Sustainable business

At $50/hour, you need 40 clients per month to hit $10K revenue. That's chaos.
At $200/hour, you need 5 clients per month. That's manageable.

Higher prices = fewer clients = more time per client = better work = more referrals = better business.

### Reason 5: Signals confidence

When you raise prices, you're saying: "I'm worth this."

Clients believe it. Your own confidence goes up. You stop taking low-value work.

## The 3-Step Process to Raise Without Losing Clients

### STEP 1: Tier Your Clients (Who do you want to keep? Who are drains?)

First, be honest about your client mix.

**Your A clients:**
- Pay on time
- Good projects
- Satisfied with results
- Have resources for growth
- No drama

**Your B clients:**
- Pay eventually
- Decent projects  
- Medium satisfaction
- Growing but resource-constrained
- Some friction

**Your C clients:**
- Slow pay
- Problem projects
- Complain a lot
- Limited budget
- High friction

**Strategy:**
- Raise prices on all A clients (they can afford it, won't leave)
- Raise prices modestly on B clients (some will leave, that's okay)
- Grandfathered or let go C clients with reasonable notice (don't want them anyway)

### STEP 2: Create a Tiered Increase Timeline (Don't Raise All at Once)

Most people raise prices once a year. This is fine. I recommend:

**Approach 1: All at once** (bold, works if you're confident)
- "Effective [date], new rate is $[new price]"
- Works for: Existing clients (they understand you're worth more)
- Risk: 10% might leave (but you gain 30% revenue increase)

**Approach 2: Tiered by contract renewal** (less disruptive, slower)
- Month 1: Don't renew with 5 worst-fit C clients
- Month 2: Raise rates 15% for contracts coming up for renewal
- Month 3: Raise rates 25% for new clients
- Month 4: Grandfathered increase for remaining clients (+10%)

**Approach 3: Grandfathering** (keeps clients happy)
- "Your rate stays at $[old price]. New clients start at $[new price]."
- Over 2-3 years, you've phased in higher pricing
- Clients feel special (they got "locked in rate")
- But you're not stuck at old price forever

I recommend **Approach 2** for most solopreneurs. It's aggressive but not reckless.

### STEP 3: The Communication (It Matters)

**Bad approach:**
- "Raising my rates to $[new price] effective [date]"
- (sounds transactional, clients feel abandoned)

**Good approach:**
- Position it as value increase ("I've helped you achieve X results")
- Give advance notice (30-90 days)
- Offer them lock-in option (stay at current price for 6 more months?)
- Connect it to their success

**Real email I sent:**

```
Subject: About your [project] results

Quick note: We've been working together for 2 years. In that time:
- Your revenue went from $[X] to $[Y]
- We've done [number] successful projects
- Your team credits our work in your case study

Because of this success, and because market rates have shifted, I'm updating 
my service pricing. Here's what it means for you:

Current rate: $[old price]
New rate (effective 90 days): $[new price]

Two options:
1. Lock in current rate for 6 more months (I'll honor it)
2. Transition to new rate on [date]

Either way, I'm excited to keep working together. You're one of my best
clients and I want to make sure we're aligned.

Let me know what questions you have.
```

This email:
- Acknowledges the relationship
- Shows the value they received
- Makes the increase about their success, not my greed
- Gives them choice
- Maintains relationship

**Result**: Zero clients left. Most picked option 1 (lock in current rate for 6 months). After 6 months, only 1 asked to leave (for budget reasons, not price resistance).

## Do This When Raising Prices

### ✓ DO:
- Give 30-90 days notice (not surprise)
- Highlight your value ("because of results I've delivered")
- Offer grandfathering option (lock in old rate briefly)
- Segment by client quality (A clients first)
- Raise prices regularly (annually, not every 5 years)

### ✗ DON'T:
- Don't apologize ("I hate to do this, but...")
- Don't hide behind "inflation" (be confidence-based, not excuse-based)
- Don't surprise clients mid-contract
- Don't underestimate your value
- Don't raise prices for bad clients (let them go instead)

## How to Raise Prices (The Smart Way)

### Method 1: Grandfathering (Best for Existing Clients)

Keep current clients at old price. New clients pay new price.

**How:**
- "I'm raising prices to $1,200/month"
- "Current clients lock in $1,000/month for life" (or "for next year")
- New clients get new rate

**Benefits:**
- No one loses anything
- Existing clients feel special
- New clients normalize higher price
- Everyone is happy

**Drawback:**
- Takes longer to raise revenue across board
- But zero resistance

### Method 2: Bracket (Good for 10+ Clients)

Raise prices in tiers.

**How:**
"I'm restructuring pricing based on scope. Three options:
- Starter: $800/month (limited scope)
- Professional: $1,200/month (full scope)
- Premium: $1,800/month (priority support + quarterly strategy)"

**Benefits:**
- Everyone sees value, moves up tiers
- Some pay more than old rate
- Doesn't feel like "price increase," feels like "better options"

### Method 3: Seasonal (Good for Project Work)

Change prices seasonally.

**How:**
"Q1-Q2 are higher demand. Pricing is:
- Q1-Q2: $5,000/project
- Q3-Q4: $4,000/project"

**Benefits:**
- Manages demand naturally
- Higher prices at peak time
- Doesn't feel unfair

### Method 4: Direct Increase (Use if Demand is High)

Raise everyone's price. No explanation needed.

**How:**
"Starting [date], new pricing is $1,200/month"

**When to use:**
- High demand (you're booked out)
- You have strong case studies
- Market rate has moved up

**Potential risk:** 
If used wrongly, might lose some clients. Use only when demand > supply.

When you need to raise prices on existing clients:

**Subject:** "Important Update on [Service] Pricing"

"Hi [name],

I wanted to let you know I'm adjusting my pricing structure effective [date].

**Here's what's changing for you:**

Current rate: $1,000/month
New rate: $1,200/month (effective [date])

**Here's why:**

Since we started working together, I've:
- Delivered [specific results] for you
- Refined my process (saving you 20% more time)
- Added [new service/value]

The market rate for this value is now $1,200-1,500/month. This adjustment reflects that.

**Your options:**

Option 1: Stay at current rate through [end date]. After that, new rate applies.
Option 2: Move to new rate immediately (and I'll credit you $200 as thanks for being an early client)
Option 3: Scale back to [limited] scope at $800/month

**My commitment to you:**

Nothing changes about service quality. You still get [what you get]. I'm just reflecting the value we've built together.

Looking forward to continuing our work.

[Your name]"

## How to Overcome Price Increase Objections

### Objection: "That's a lot of increase"

Response: "I understand. Here's the value you're getting: [results]. The market rate for that is $1,500/month. $1,200 is actually below market."

### Objection: "Can you justify that?"

Response: "Sure. Here's what's changed: [your results for them], [new value you added], [improved process]."

### Objection: "I'll have to shop around"

Response: "Totally fair. I'd actually recommend it. What I'm charging reflects the market rate for this value. If you find better elsewhere, go for it. But I think you'll find similar pricing at quality level."

### Objection: "I thought we had a long-term arrangement"

Response: "We do. I'm committed to your success. This just brings pricing in line with market value. [Offer grandfathering option] Can we stay at current rate through [date]?"
