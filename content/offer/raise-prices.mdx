# How to Raise Prices Without Losing Clients

Most solopreneurs don't raise prices. They stay at the same rate for years. Meanwhile, inflation is 3-5% annually.

You're actually getting PAID LESS each year.

Plus, lower prices attract less committed clients. Higher prices attract serious ones.

Raising prices is not evil. It's necessary.

---

## Why You Should Raise Prices

### Reason 1: You're Worth More Now

When you started, you had 0 experience. Now you have customers, case studies, results.

You're more valuable. Charge accordingly.

### Reason 2: Inflation

If you charged $1,000 in Year 1, you need $1,035 in Year 2 just to stay even.

Most solopreneurs don't raise prices. So they're making less in real dollars.

### Reason 3: Better Quality Clients

Higher price = more serious clients

Low price = clients who ping you constantly, negotiate everything, don't respect your time

### Reason 4: Sustainable Business

Low prices = burnout

At $50/hr, you need 40 clients per month to hit $10k revenue.
At $200/hr, you need 5 clients per month.

Which is sustainable?

### Reason 5: More Profit

If you raise prices 25% and lose 10% of clients, you make 12.5% more money.

Spoiler: You won't lose that many.

---

## The Math of Price Increases

**Current situation:**
- 10 active clients
- $1,000/month per client
- $10,000/month revenue

**If you raise prices 20%** ($1,000 → $1,200) **and lose 1 client:**
- 9 clients
- $1,200/month per client
- $10,800/month revenue

You actually make more money with fewer clients.

**If you raise prices 25%** ($1,000 → $1,250) **and lose 2 clients:**
- 8 clients
- $1,250/month per client
- $10,000/month revenue

Break even, but with less work.

Most solopreneurs don't lose any clients. They might lose 5-10%.

## When to Raise Prices

### Raise Price When:

**1. You Have Results**
Case studies, testimonials, proven results → you can charge more

**2. Demand is High**
People are reaching out, booking is hard to schedule → you can charge more

**3. You're Doing More**
Your service has improved, you've added value → you can charge more

**4. Market Has Moved**
Competitors have raised prices → raise yours too

**5. You're Frustrated**
You feel undervalued or overworked → raise prices

**Don't Raise Price When:**
- No one knows who you are
- You have zero case studies
- You're new to market
- Demand is low

## How to Raise Prices (The Smart Way)

### Method 1: Grandfathering (Best for Existing Clients)

Keep current clients at old price. New clients pay new price.

**How:**
- "I'm raising prices to $1,200/month"
- "Current clients lock in $1,000/month for life" (or "for next year")
- New clients get new rate

**Benefits:**
- No one loses anything
- Existing clients feel special
- New clients normalize higher price
- Everyone is happy

**Drawback:**
- Takes longer to raise revenue across board
- But zero resistance

### Method 2: Bracket (Good for 10+ Clients)

Raise prices in tiers.

**How:**
"I'm restructuring pricing based on scope. Three options:
- Starter: $800/month (limited scope)
- Professional: $1,200/month (full scope)
- Premium: $1,800/month (priority support + quarterly strategy)"

**Benefits:**
- Everyone sees value, moves up tiers
- Some pay more than old rate
- Doesn't feel like "price increase," feels like "better options"

### Method 3: Seasonal (Good for Project Work)

Change prices seasonally.

**How:**
"Q1-Q2 are higher demand. Pricing is:
- Q1-Q2: $5,000/project
- Q3-Q4: $4,000/project"

**Benefits:**
- Manages demand naturally
- Higher prices at peak time
- Doesn't feel unfair

### Method 4: Direct Increase (Use if Demand is High)

Raise everyone's price. No explanation needed.

**How:**
"Starting [date], new pricing is $1,200/month"

**When to use:**
- High demand (you're booked out)
- You have strong case studies
- Market rate has moved up

**Potential risk:** 
If used wrongly, might lose some clients. Use only when demand > supply.

## The Price Increase Conversation

When you need to raise prices on existing clients:

### The Email/Message

**Subject:** "Important Update on [Service] Pricing"

"Hi [name],

I wanted to let you know I'm adjusting my pricing structure effective [date].

**Here's what's changing for you:**

Current rate: $1,000/month
New rate: $1,200/month (effective [date])

**Here's why:**

Since we started working together, I've:
- Delivered [specific results] for you
- Refined my process (saving you 20% more time)
- Added [new service/value]

The market rate for this value is now $1,200-1,500/month. This adjustment reflects that.

**Your options:**

Option 1: Stay at current rate through [end date]. After that, new rate applies.
Option 2: Move to new rate immediately (and I'll credit you $200 as thanks for being an early client)
Option 3: Scale back to [limited] scope at $800/month

**My commitment to you:**

Nothing changes about service quality. You still get [what you get]. I'm just reflecting the value we've built together.

Looking forward to continuing our work.

[Your name]"

### The Phone Call (If Needed)

"Thanks for getting on the call. I wanted to discuss something directly.

I'm raising my rates starting [date]. Here's what that means for you:

You're paying $1,000/month. Starting [date], it's $1,200/month.

I wanted to let you know directly because you're important to me.

Here's why I'm doing it: [mention their results, how you've improved]

How does that feel?"

## How to Overcome Price Increase Objections

### Objection: "That's a lot of increase"

Response: "I understand. Here's the value you're getting: [results]. The market rate for that is $1,500/month. $1,200 is actually below market."

### Objection: "Can you justify that?"

Response: "Sure. Here's what's changed: [your results for them], [new value you added], [improved process]."

### Objection: "I'll have to shop around"

Response: "Totally fair. I'd actually recommend it. What I'm charging reflects the market rate for this value. If you find better elsewhere, go for it. But I think you'll find similar pricing at quality level."

### Objection: "I thought we had a long-term arrangement"

Response: "We do. I'm committed to your success. This just brings pricing in line with market value. [Offer grandfathering option] Can we stay at current rate through [date]?"

## Signs You Should Raise Prices Soon

- You're booked out (can't take more clients)
- You have 5+ case studies
- Your testimonials are glowing
- People are saying "yes" without negotiating
- You feel undercompensated
- You're raising prices less than inflation
- You haven't raised prices in 12+ months

If 3+ apply to you: Raise prices now.

---

## The Price Increase Psychology

**Perception matters more than reality.**

Raising prices from $1,000 to $1,200 (20% increase) feels big.

But if you position it as:
- "Adjusted for inflation + value increase"
- "Now includes [new benefit]"
- "Market rate for this is $1,500"

It feels reasonable.

Same increase, different framing.

## The Rule of Price Increases

Raise prices 15-25% every 12-18 months.

At 20% every year, in 3 years you're at $1,728 (from $1,000).
That's $8,728 more per year at same client load.

It's how you build wealth as a solopreneur.

Don't stay at starting price forever.

Your value increased. Your price should too.
