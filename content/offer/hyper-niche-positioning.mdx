## The Pattern I'm Seeing

Most solopreneurs are broke.

And I'm watching the rich ones do something most people get completely backwards.

Every solopreneur I see pulling in five figures monthly, six figures annually—they're all doing the same thing. But it's the opposite of what conventional wisdom teaches.

The conventional wisdom says: "Start broad. Appeal to everyone. Then narrow down once you find what works."

Wrong.

The pattern says: Start insanely specific. Own everything in that narrow slice. Expand later if you want.

Here's what I mean.

## The Generalist Trap

Let me show you two businesses.

**Business A:** "I'm a web designer. I help businesses with websites."

**Business B:** "I build Webflow sites for SaaS startups."

Same person, potentially. Same skills. Same tools. Same work.

Different outcomes.

Business A gets ignored. Nobody searches for "a web designer." They search for specific pain. Business A sounds like 10,000 other people, so nobody picks up the phone.

Business B gets booked. SaaS founders know exactly what they're getting. No confusion. No shopping around. No "let me think about it." They need a Webflow expert for SaaS, they found one, they hire.


And the pricing? Business A charges $50-100/hour. Business B charges $150-250/hour. Or $5,000-20,000 per project. With better margins and faster closes.

Same skill, different audience — and much higher income.

This isn't luck. This isn't because they're smarter or more talented. It's because specificity wins. Specificity sounds like expertise. Specificity repels people who aren't your ideal client, which is exactly what you want.

## Real Hyper-Niche Examples (2025-2026)

Let me show you what winning looks like.

**Notion Setup for Real Estate Agents**

Real estate agents want systems. They want to be organized. But they don't want to spend three months building a Notion workspace from scratch. Enter the specialist: someone who sets up Notion specifically for real estate teams.

The positioning: "Notion systems for real estate brokerages."

Income: $3,000-$10,000 per setup. Often $500/month ongoing support. Market size? There are 2+ million real estate agents globally. How many of them have heard of this specialist? Maybe 50-100 actively marketing it. You do the math.

**AI Prompt Engineering for SaaS Startups**

SaaS founders want to leverage AI. They get it intellectually. But most don't know how to actually use it—how to structure prompts, how to integrate it into their product, how to train their team on it.


The hyper-niche specialist steps in: "AI prompt engineering for SaaS teams."

Income: $5,000-$15,000 per engagement. Repeatable, since every SaaS company needs this. And the research backs this: many solopreneurs reported revenue growth after niching in 2025.

**LinkedIn Content Strategy for B2B Coaches**

Coaches need clients. They know LinkedIn is where their clients hang out. But they don't have time to figure out the platform. The specialist: "LinkedIn growth strategy for B2B coaches."

Income: $2,000-$8,000/month per retainer. Why? Because coaches have revenue-based income. Commission checks hit, they invest in growth.

Notice the pattern in each example?

**[Specific Skill] + [Specific Tool/Method] + [Specific Audience] = Your Niche**

Not "I'm a designer." → "I build Webflow sites for SaaS startups."

Not "I do marketing." → "I create LinkedIn strategy for coaches."

Not "I'm a consultant." → "I set up AI automation for e-commerce brands."

The three-dimensional specificity is what creates the competitive moat.

## The 3-Dimensional Niche Matrix

Let me give you the framework.

Most solopreneurs think of niching as one-dimensional. They pick a skill and hope.

Wrong dimension.

The winning formula has three dimensions working together:

**Dimension 1: Your Core Skill**

What can you actually do? What are you credible talking about?

- Web design
- Content creation
- AI automation
- Notion setup
- Email marketing
- Social media strategy

Pick the one you're genuinely good at.

**Dimension 2: How You Do It (Your Tool or Method)**

This is where most people miss the power.

Instead of being a "content creator," you become a "LinkedIn content strategist." Same skill, but now it's specific to a platform. You're the expert in LinkedIn. Not on content in general.

Instead of "I do automation," you become the "Make.com expert for e-commerce." Now you're not just an automation person—you're the person who knows Make specifically.

The specificity in your method is a differentiator.

- Webflow (not generic web design)
- LinkedIn (not social media)
- Make or n8n (not "automation in general")
- AI (not vague consulting)

**Dimension 3: Your Audience**

Who specifically do you serve?

Not "businesses." Not "entrepreneurs." Specific.

- SaaS startups (funded, have budget, serious problems)
- Real estate agents (consistent income, problem-aware)
- B2B coaches (revenue-based, ready to invest)
- E-commerce brands (specific pain point, defined success metric)

Now combine all three.

**[Webflow] + [SaaS Startups] = Webflow specialist for SaaS founders**

**[LinkedIn] + [B2B Coaches] = LinkedIn strategist for coaches**

**[Make] + [E-commerce] = Make expert for Shopify store owners**

Here's why this works mathematically.

Generic "web designer" has 10,000 competitors. You get lost.

Semi-niche "Webflow designer" has 500 competitors. Better, but still crowded.

Hyper-niche "Webflow for SaaS startups"? Try 15-20 active competitors globally. 99% less competition.

Result: Your ideal client finds you. Conversion rates skyrocket. Pricing power increases. You go from invisible to the obvious choice.

## How to Find Your Hyper-Niche in Four Steps

Stop overthinking. Do this.

**Step 1: List Your Core Skills**

What are you credible talking about? What could you teach someone without feeling like a fraud?

Write down 5-7 skills. For OneFoundr's audience, common ones are:

- Building solo businesses
- Creating digital products or offers
- Marketing without a big team
- Selling without a sales team
- Automation and systems
- AI tools and workflows

Pick the skill that energizes you. This matters more than you think.

**Step 2: List Audiences You Understand (Or Have Served)**

Who do you genuinely understand? Not theoretically—actually.

Have you worked with them? Do you hang out in their communities? Do you see their specific problems daily?

Examples: SaaS founders, real estate agents, e-commerce brands, B2B coaches, indie hackers, solo founders, freelancers, creators, agency owners.

Write down 5-7 audiences.

**Step 3: Map to 2025-2026 Demand**

What problems are urgent RIGHT NOW in your potential audience?

Use AI to research fast. Ask ChatGPT or Claude:
- "What are the biggest problems for [audience] in 2026?"
- "What tools are [audience] struggling to implement?"
- "What's the #1 pain point for [audience] right now?"

Look for:
- Emerging tools they're trying to use
- Regulatory changes hitting them
- Specific bottlenecks mentioned repeatedly
- Market shifts forcing new behaviors

Marked growth in specialized productized services shows clear demand. You just need to find the gap.

**Step 4: Validate in Seven Days**

Here's where most people fail. They never actually ask the market.

Pick your top 3 niche combinations. DM or email 10 people in each niche. Real people. On LinkedIn, Twitter, wherever they hang out.

Use this template:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I noticed you [specific observation about their work]. I'm researching [your niche topic] and I'd love your perspective:
>
> What's your biggest challenge with [specific problem]?
>
> Would love to hear your thoughts.

That's it. Watch what happens.

If you get 3+ enthusiastic responses? That's your signal. That niche is real and they want the solution.

If you get silence or generic replies? Wrong niche. Try the next one.

The market tells you everything if you listen.

## Pricing Power of Hyper-Niche

Here's why this matters financially.

**Generic Web Designer:**
- Rate: $50-75/hour
- Project size: $2,500-5,000
- Conversion rate: 5% of inquiries
- Monthly income ceiling: $5,000-10,000

**Semi-Niche Webflow Designer:**
- Rate: $100-150/hour
- Project size: $7,500-15,000
- Conversion rate: 15% of inquiries
- Monthly income ceiling: $15,000-25,000

**Hyper-Niche Webflow for SaaS Startups:**
- Rate: $150-250+/hour (or $5K-20K per project)
- Project size: $15,000-50,000+
- Conversion rate: 40-50% of inquiries
- Monthly income ceiling: $30,000-100,000+

Same person. Same hours. Five to ten times the income.

Why does hyper-niche command premium pricing?

First: Specificity reduces risk. Your client doesn't hire a "designer." They hire the Webflow expert for SaaS. They know exactly what they're getting.

Second: Scarcity creates value. There are 10,000 generic web designers. There are 15 Webflow experts for SaaS startups. Fewer options means higher pricing power.

Third: Your ideal client has real budget. SaaS startups are funded. E-commerce brands have revenue. Real estate agents have commission income. These audiences aren't price-shopping. They're looking for competence.

Fourth: The close is faster. Your ideal client isn't comparing five options. They found the specialist, they trust it solves their problem, they say yes.

The math is simple. Five prospects to one client at $5,000 = $5,000/month. Same time invested? Five prospects to four clients at $5,000? That's $20,000/month.

Hyper-niche doesn't just feel better. It pays better.

## Addressing the Objections You're Having Right Now

**"What if I niche too small and run out of clients?"**

The market is bigger than you think. SaaS startups alone? 25,000+ in the US. Millions globally. You're not running out of clients.

The real question: Can you stay booked? Most hyper-niche specialists worry about having too many inquiries, not too few.

**"What if I get bored?"**

Fair question. The answer: You can pivot. Master one niche, dominate it, then add adjacent offerings to the same audience. You've already built trust.

Or here's the reframe: "Boring to you" often means "stable and lucrative." Boring pays bills. Variety doesn't.

**"But I have multiple skills. Can't I offer them all?"**

Start with ONE niche. Master it. Once you're booked solid, you expand. You don't start with everything. You start focused, then add.

**"Isn't being a specialist too limiting?"**

No. Being a generalist is limiting. Generalists are invisible.

Specialists get found, get hired, get premium rates. Research shows many solopreneurs grew revenue after niching; very few regret being too specific.
