## The 90-Day Launch is Dead

Most startup advice tells you the same thing: spend 90 days planning, building, and validating before you launch.

Here's the problem with that timeline:

- Motivation dies around week 6
- You've spent 3 months on something nobody asked for
- You discover on day 89 that your [target market (the one you chose from business ideas)](/start/business-ideas) doesn't want it
- You're out of money with nothing to show

Solopreneurs don't have 90 days. You have maybe 4-6 weeks of momentum before doubt sets in.

The better approach: use AI to compress the timeline to 30 days. Get to market, get real feedback, and know if you're onto something *before* you invest real money.

## The 30-Day Launch Formula

This isn't about cutting corners. It's about priority. You launch with 60% of what you think you need, get market feedback, then build the other 40% based on what customers actually want.

### Week 1: Validate the Problem

**Goal:** Confirm that your target customer actually has this problem (and cares enough to pay for it).

**Day 1-2: Define your target customer**
- Who is one specific person you're building this for?
- What's their biggest challenge (related to your idea)?
- Why do they have this challenge?
- What are they doing now to solve it?

Use AI: Claude or ChatGPT to interview your target customer. Feed them the problem, have the model roleplay as your customer, ask questions.

**Day 3-5: Talk to 5-10 potential customers**
- Informal conversations (not surveys)
- Ask about their problem, not about your solution
- Listen for pain points they mention without prompting
- Note: "Would you pay for a solution?" matters less than "How much time/money does this problem cost you?"

Use AI: Have AI draft interview questions and help you synthesize common themes from conversations.

**Day 6-7: Decision point**
- Do 5+ people confirm this is a real problem they're currently trying to solve?
- If yes: move to Week 2
- If no: pivot or pause

### Week 2: Build the MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

**Goal:** Get a working version live that solves the core problem (not all problems, just the core one).

**What you're building:**
- If it's a service: a landing page + booking link + [pre-defined productized offer](/offer/productized-services) that customers understand immediately
- If it's a course: 3-5 core modules (not 12)
- If it's software: the 1-2 core features (not the full feature suite)
- If it's a [digital product](/offer/digital-products): a basic version that solves the main problem

**How AI speeds this up:**

- **Landing page**: Use Claude or ChatGPT to generate copy from your research. Feed it customer pain points, have it write 5 variations. Pick the best. (4 hours instead of 20)
- **Content**: Use AI to draft 80% of educational content. You edit for your voice and specific examples. (15 hours instead of 40)
- **Design**: Use Webflow AI or similar to generate layouts. Customize to match your brand. (8 hours instead of 30)
- **Processes**: Use AI to outline your service delivery process. Map out exactly what customers get. (2 hours instead of meetings)

**Days 8-14: Build**
- Put 2-3 hours daily into building the MVP
- Use templates and AI tools (don't code from scratch)
- Focus on getting something live, not perfect

**Real timeline:**
- Landing page: 4 hours
- Core content/service offering: 8-12 hours
- Payment setup: 2 hours
- Testing: 2 hours
- Total: ~20 hours of work across 7 days

You have something launchable by end of week 2.

### Week 3: Launch and Get First Customers

**Goal:** Get 3-5 paying customers and gather feedback.

**Day 15-17: Launch**
- Share with your network (email, Slack, Discord, LinkedIn)
- Be honest about being early/MVP status
- Offer a launch special (50% off, or free first month, or payment after they see results)
- Ask for feedback ruthlessly

**Day 18-21: Get first customers**
- Contact 20-30 people directly who fit your ideal customer profile
- Don't pitch. Explain what you're building and ask if they want to be early users.
- Aim for 3-5 "yes" answers
- They should be paying (even at a discount), not free users

Use AI: Generate personalized outreach messages based on profiles of your targets.

**Why this matters:**
- Real customers give real feedback, not hypothetical questions
- You learn product-market fit indicators
- You get testimonials and case studies

### Week 4: Validate and Iterate

**Goal:** Gather feedback, fix the biggest problems, validate you're solving the right problem.

**Day 22-28: Work with first customers**
- If it's a service: deliver and track results
- If it's a course: get customer feedback via survey or calls
- If it's software: watch customers use it, note where they struggle
- If it's a product: get testimonials on what worked, what didn't

**Key question:** Are customers achieving the result you promised?
- Yes? You're probably onto something. Invest more.
- Partially? You nailed the problem but missed the solution. Iterate the solution.
- No? You might have the wrong target customer or wrong problem. Pivot or pause.

**Day 29-30: Decide**
- Keep building (you know people want this)
- Adjust the offering (problem is right, solution needs work)
- Pause (wrong problem, wrong customer, or good idea but bad timing)

## Tools That Make This Faster

AI tools that compress the 90-day timeline to 30 days:

**Research & Interviewing:**
- Claude/ChatGPT: roleplay as customers, synthesize feedback
- Typeform or Google Forms: quick surveys
- Calendly: schedule customer calls

**Landing Pages & Copy:**
- AI landing page generators (Unbounce AI, Browse AI)
- ChatGPT: generate landing page copy variations
- Figma: design mockups quickly

**Content Creation:**
- ChatGPT: draft learning materials, outlines, frameworks
- Decks.ai: generate presentation slides from text
- Adobe Firefly: generate simple graphics

**Service Delivery:**
- Notion: template-based processes and deliverables
- Zapier: automate workflows
- Loom: record how-to videos quickly

**Email & Outreach:**
- Gmail templates
- Clay or Hunter: find email addresses
- ChatGPT: personalized outreach variations

## Common Mistakes in the 30-Day Launch

**1. Building before validating**
- Wrong: Spend all 30 days building the perfect solution
- Right: Spend week 1 validating, then spend weeks 2-4 building + selling

**2. Aiming for perfection**
- Wrong: Launch when you're "ready" (you never are)
- Right: Launch at 60% quality with a clear "this is beta" message

**3. Not charging**
- Wrong: Free trial or free version to "get feedback"
- Right: Charge something (even a discount) so you know they're serious

**4. Targeting everyone**
- Wrong: "This is for anyone who wants to [benefit]"
- Right: "This is for [specific person with specific problem]"

**5. Ignoring feedback**
- Wrong: Launch and then radio silence
- Right: Launch, gather feedback daily, adjust

## Real Examples: 30-Day Launches That Worked

**Example 1: Productized Service**
- Week 1: Explored 3 specific copywriting problems, talked to 8 corporate founders
- Week 2: Built landing page + defined exact service (email copy audit, not "general copywriting help")
- Week 3: 4 customers at $200/month
- Week 4: All 4 customers reported 15-25% email open rate increase, 2 referred others
- Outcome: Booked for 3 months, used customer feedback to build full offering

**Example 2: Online Course**
- Week 1: Validated 10 solopreneurs wanted help with cold email
- Week 2: Created 4-module course using framework from existing guides + repurposed content
- Week 3: Launched at $49, got 12 people enrolled in first week
- Week 4: Synthesized feedback (wanted more personalization examples), added 2 modules
- Outcome: Launched full course 6 weeks later, 60+ enrolled, $3K revenue

**Example 3: SaaS**
- Week 1: Talked to 15 small agency owners about their time tracking problem
- Week 2: Built Zapier automation that solved the core problem (99% of the utility with 5% of the code)
- Week 3: 3 customers at $49/month
- Week 4: Feedback showed they also wanted export features. Built in week 5-6.
- Outcome: Validated problem + solution, now investing in proper app

## The Psychology of 30-Day Launches

Here's what actually happens in most founders' heads:

**Days 1-7:** Excitement and momentum ("I'm doing this!")
**Days 8-21:** Doubt and comparison ("Is this actually viable?")
**Days 22-35:** Either validation or burnout

By launching at day 15, you're launching *during* doubt but *before* burnout. Real customer feedback in weeks 3-4 either confirms you (turns doubt into conviction) or shows you should pivot (prevents months of wrong direction).

This is psychologically smarter than the 90-day approach, which keeps you in doubt for 3 months.

## The 30-Day Launch Checklist

**Week 1 (Validate):**
- [ ] Target customer clearly defined
- [ ] 5-10 conversations with potential customers completed
- [ ] Problem confirmed as real and worth solving
- [ ] Decision to proceed made

**Week 2 (Build):**
- [ ] MVP scope clearly defined (what's included, what's not)
- [ ] Landing page drafted and live
- [ ] Service/product/course core offering ready
- [ ] Payment setup working

**Week 3 (Launch):**
- [ ] Announced to network (email, social, direct)
- [ ] 3-5 early customers acquired
- [ ] Early customer feedback collected

**Week 4 (Iterate):**
- [ ] Customer feedback synthesized
- [ ] Quick improvements made (if needed)
- [ ] Decision made: keep building, pivot, or pause

If you check all these boxes, you've compressed the startup timeline and validated your idea before betting heavy.

This isn't the end. It's the beginning.
