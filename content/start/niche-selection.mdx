# Niche Selection: Find Your Profitable Sweet Spot

Your niche is who you serve + what problem you solve.

Most people choose their niche wrong. They pick something too broad, or with no demand, or that doesn't interest them.

A good niche is:
- **Narrow enough** to dominate
- **Profitable** (people have money)
- **Hungry** (they actively want help)
- **Interesting to you** (you'll do this for years)

---

## The Niche Paradox

Intuition says: "Go broad, reach more people"

Truth: "Go narrow, become the obvious choice"

When you say "I help businesses," no one pays attention.

When you say "I help SaaS founders optimize pricing," everyone notices.

Narrow wins. Always.

---

## The 3 Levels of Niche

### Level 1: Industry
- Software Companies
- E-commerce
- Coaches

### Level 2: Role/Challenge
- B2B SaaS Founders
- Freelance designers
- Solo consultants
- Agency owners

### Level 3: Specific Problem
- Seed-stage SaaS founders struggling to find PMF
- Designers charging too little, want to raise prices
- Solo consultants wanting to build a team
- Agencies wanting to scale beyond themselves

**Your niche should be Levels 2-3. Not Level 1.**

---

## How to Find a Profitable Niche

### Step 1: Brainstorm Your Backgrounds

List:
- Industries you know
- Problems you've solved
- Results you've gotten
- Failures you've had (often the best insights)
- Things you're passionate about

Example:
- Worked in SaaS for 8 years
- Built features nobody wanted
- Then learned product-market fit
- Got frustrated with designers being underpaid
- Love mentoring people

Potential niches from this: SaaS founders, designers wanting to raise prices, designers wanting to transition to product roles.

### Step 2: Identify Specific Problems You Solve

For each background, what problems did you solve?

SaaS experience → I solved PMF problem
Mentoring → I solved confidence/knowledge problem

What specific, measurable outcome?


**Facebook Groups:**
(Ahrefs, SEMrush) How many searches for related terms? (Target 1000+ searches/month minimum)


## Red Flags for Bad Niches
Example: "Starving college students" = passion but no budget
### Red Flag 3: Too Crowded
Everyone and their mother is doing it. You'll never stand out.
Example: "Life coaches," "Instagram coaches"

### Red Flag 4: Doesn't Interest You
You picked it for money, not passion.
You'll hate it in 6 months.

### Red Flag 5: Problem Not Painful Enough
People have the problem, but it's not expensive to fix.
Example: "helping people organize their socks" - not painful enough to pay

## Competition Analysis

For your niche, ask:

1. **Who are the top 5 people serving this niche?**
   - Google "[niche] help" or "[niche] coaching"
   - Look at top 5 results

2. **What are they doing?**
   - How do they position?
   - What do they charge?
   - How do they market?

3. **What are they missing?**
   - What complaints do they get?
   - What's not working well?
   - Where's the gap?

Your angle = their gap.

Example:
- Most design coaches teach "raise your hourly rate"
- Gap: They don't teach moving from hourly to value-based
- Your angle: "How to transition to project pricing and 3x your income"

## The Niche Timeline

**Month 1:** Research 5-10 potential niches

**Month 2:** Validate top 3 (talk to 10 people in each)

**Month 3:** Commit to 1 niche

**Months 4-12:** Focus entirely on that niche

**Year 2+:** Either double down or pivot if needed

Once you pick, commit for 12 months minimum. Don't hop around.

---

## Niche Pivot Permission

It's okay to pivot your niche if:

**Good Reasons to Pivot:**
- You misread the demand (no one's buying)
- You hate it after 6 months
- Better opportunity emerged
- Your expertise shifted

**Bad Reasons to Pivot:**

- It's hard (it always is at first)
- First client didn't work out
- Didn't get rich in 90 days
- Found another shiny niche

If you're pivoting every 3 months, your problem isn't the niche. It's commitment or execution.

**This Week:**
