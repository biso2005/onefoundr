# Discovery Calls: Converting 40-50% of Discovery Calls to Clients

My first year, I was terrible at discovery calls.

I talked 70% of the time. I pitched before understanding their problem. I tried to convince skeptics. I negotiated on price immediately.

Close rate: 15%. 1 in 6 calls became a client.

Then I changed my approach based on one principle: **The best sales call moves the prospect from unclear to clarity.**

I stopped selling. I started diagnosing.

My new close rate: 45%. 1 in 2 calls becomes a client.

This is the exact structure, psychology, and scripts I use on every call.

---

## The Pre-Call Setup (You Win Before the Call Starts)

### STEP 1: The Intake Form (Filter Out 50% of Bad Fits)

Before anyone books, they must fill a 3-minute form. I use Typeform.

**My exact intake questions:**

1. "What's your biggest business challenge right now?" (Open-ended, listen to language)
2. "What have you already tried to solve this?" (Shows sophistication level + resources available)
3. "If you could wave a magic wand and solve this, what would your business look like in 90 days?" (Reveals their outcome hunger)
4. "What's your monthly/annual revenue right now?" (Budget proxy)
5. "Are you the decision-maker, or do you need to consult with anyone else?" (BANT: Authority)
6. "How long has this been a problem?" (Urgency proxy)
7. "When would you want to start working on this?" (Timeline)

**What this does:**
- Filters out people who can't afford you (answer question 4)
- Identifies urgency (questions 6-7)
- Shows if they've thought about the problem (question 2)
- Reveals their desired outcome clearly (question 3)

**Result**: I only take calls with 5/7 strong answers. People with weak answers get a "nurture email" + put in follow-up sequence.

### STEP 2: 10-Minute Pre-Call Research (Be Dangerous Smart)

Before I get on the call, I spend 10 minutes learning about them:

1. **LinkedIn review** (2 min):
   - Job titles (CEO = decision-maker, VP = maybe)
   - Recent posts/activity (understand them as person)
   - Company size (shapes what solutions they need)

2. **Quick Google** (2 min):
   - Company website (understand their business)
   - Competitors (understand their market context)
   - Recent news (are they growing or struggling?)

3. **Re-read their intake form** (2 min):
   - Identify 3 assumptions they expressed
   - Prepare 2-3 specific questions I'll ask
   - Note contradictions ("says growth is goal, but revenue is flat" = likely money mindset issue)

4. **Personal context** (2 min):
   - Mutual connections on LinkedIn? Mention it
   - Did they find me via specific content? Reference it
   - Any shared context? Voice it early

**Example of what this looks like:**

Their form: "Biggest challenge is getting clients consistently"

My research: They're in design, 3 employees, flat revenue last 2 years, posted once on LinkedIn 4 months ago

My prepared questions:
- "You mentioned getting clients consistently — walk me through what you've tried"
- "I noticed your Instagram is inactive. How are you currently finding new business?"
- "You mentioned wanting to hit $1M revenue. How are you planning to get there with current client load?"

When I ask these *specific* questions (not generic ones), they sit back and think: "This person gets it."

## The 30-Minute Discovery Call Structure (Tested on 200+ Calls)

I run every discovery call exactly like this:

### MINUTES 0–2: FRAME THE CALL (Set Context, Reduce Pressure)

Goal: Establish confidence + shared expectations

**My exact opening:**

> "Thanks for planning time with me. Before we jump in, I want to be clear about what this call is for. This is a 30-minute conversation where I'm going to ask questions to understand your situation, and you'll get a clear sense of whether we might be a good fit working together. By the end, we'll both know if it makes sense to move forward. If the answer is 'no,' or 'not yet,' that's completely fine — I only want to work with people it's genuinely right for. Sound good?"

**What this does:**
- Removes pressure from them (they don't have to say yes)
- Frames me as diagnostician, not salesman
- Sets 30-minute boundary (keeps call efficient)
- Makes "saying no" feel safe

**Their response**: 99% of people say "sounds good." They relax.

### MINUTES 2–15: DIAGNOSE THE PROBLEM (Them Talking 80%, Me Talking 20%)

This is the core. I ask 12-14 specific questions in this section. I don't interrupt. I listen.

**The questioning sequence I use:**

**Round 1: Understand Situation** (3 questions)
1. "Walk me through what's happening right now with [specific challenge from their form]"
2. "When did you first notice this problem?"
3. "What does a typical week/day look like with this problem happening?"

*Their answers show: actual scope of problem, how it affects operations, what workarounds they're using*

**Round 2: Quantify Impact** (3 questions)
4. "If I'm hearing you right, [summarize what they said]. Is that accurate?"
5. "What's the financial impact of this? Like, when you look at lost revenue or wasted time, what number comes to mind?"
6. "Besides money, what else is this costing you? Your sanity? Your time with family? What?"

*Their answers show: if they understand the true cost of inaction, emotional AND financial*

**Round 3: Explore Attempts** (2 questions)
7. "What have you already tried to solve this?"
8. "What happened there? Why didn't it work?"

*Their answers show: they've thought seriously (or not), they've invested (or not), they understand obstacles (or not)*

**Round 4: Uncover Urgency** (2 questions)
9. "On a scale of 1-10, how urgent is fixing this right now?"
10. "What would a 10 look like? What needs to be true for this to be a 10?"

*Their answers show: if they're coasting (5-6) or dying (9-10). You don't close low-urgency leads.*

**Round 5: Explore Constraints** (2 questions)
11. "What concerns do you have about working with someone to solve this?"
12. "Is there anything that might prevent you from moving forward, in terms of budget or timing or authority?"

*Their answers surface objections BEFORE your pitch. So you can address them proactively.*

**Round 6: Clarify Desired Outcome** (1 question)
13. "If we solve this and it works out perfectly, what changes in your business?"

*Their answer: Use it verbatim when you pitch. "You said X. That's exactly what we help people achieve."*

**The Psychology Here:**

By asking 12+ questions, they do 80% of the talking. They convince themselves of the problem They hear themselves articulate pain. They realize they're serious about solving it.

When YOU pitch later, they're already in the mental space of "I need to solve this." You're not pushing. You're providing the solution to a problem they've already convinced themselves is real.

---

### MINUTES 15–22: PRESENT YOUR SOLUTION (5-7 minutes, Specific + Linked)

Only after they've fully diagnosed the problem do I present.

**My exact pitch formula:**

**Part 1: Reflect back what I heard** (1 min)

> "So based on what you've shared, here's how I'm understanding it. You're in [situation]. The main constraint is [problem they named]. And if you solve it, you get [outcome they named]. Is that right?"

They say yes. I've just anchored them to their own pain.

**Part 2: Present solution simply** (2-3 min)

> "Here's how we typically help people in your situation. [Describe actual process/methodology in 3-4 steps]. The goal is [specific outcome]. For someone in your situation with [their specific context], that typically takes [timeline] and costs [price]."

**Notice what I'm NOT doing:**
- Not listing features
- Not explaining why I'm good
- Not pitching credentials
- Not being generic

I'm saying "for YOUR situation, with YOUR constraints, here's HOW we help, what OUTCOME, what COST"

**Part 3: Show a relevant result** (1 min)

> "A similar client — also [company type], also dealing with [their problem] — went from [starting point] to [outcome] in [timeline]. That's what's possible."

Use their language. One specific example beats ten generic benefits.

---

### MINUTES 22–28: HANDLE OBJECTIONS & CLOSE (6 minutes)

After my pitch, I ask:

> "Does this seem like it could be the right fit for you?"

Then I STOP TALKING. This silence is gold. They'll either say:

**A) "Yes, let's do it"** → Skip to closing (next section)

**B) "I want to think about it"** → Objection handling

**C) "It's more expensive than I thought"** → Objection handling

**D) "I'm not sure it's the right time"** → Objection handling

## The 3 Objections & How I Handle Them

### Objection 1: "I need to think about it"

This is the most common. 99% of the time, it means "I have a concern I haven't voiced."

**My response:**

> "Of course. I appreciate that. Can I ask — what's making you want to think about it? Is it the investment level, or is it a confidence thing about whether it'll actually work? Because those are very different."

If investment: "What would the investment need to be for you to move forward?"

If confidence: "What would help you feel more confident about this? Is there something I can explain differently?"

Usually they say something like "I'm not sure it'll work for my situation" or "I need to talk to my partner."

I address that specific thing right then. Not with pressure. With problem-solving.

> "I hear you on talking to your partner. Here's what I suggest: either we do a quick call with both of you tomorrow, or I send you a one-page overview you can share and discuss. Which would be easier?"

This moves the stall into a concrete next step.

### Objection 2: "It's more expensive than I expected"

This sounds like a price objection. It's usually not.

**My response:**

> "I hear you — this is an investment. Let me ask: is the expense the issue, or is it a confidence thing? Like, if you were 100% sure this would work, would the investment feel reasonable?"

If they say "yeah, if I'm sure it works, it's fine," then it's a confidence issue, not a price issue. I go back to painting the outcome picture.

If they say "no, it's actually too much," then it's a real budget constraint. I ask:

> "What would feel right to you?"

If they name a lower price, I either:
1. Offer a smaller, lower-price package ("We could do Phase 1 for $X, then Phase 2 later")
2. Move them to a waitlist ("I can't go lower on my core offering, but I want to work with you. Here's another option...")
3. Say no and refer them elsewhere ("This might not be right, but I know someone who...")

Saying no to bad-fit clients at the price-objection stage is better than one bad engagement.

### Objection 3: "I'm not sure it's the right time"

This is usually a brush-off. But sometimes it's real.

**My response:**

> "What would need to be true for it to be the right time? Like, what's the blocker?"

Real answer: "I'm closing a deal in 2 weeks and want to focus on that first"
→ "Perfect. Let's plan to start [date]. I'll send you a contract now, and we kick off then. Does that work?"

Brush-off answer: "I don't know, just not right now"
→ "Totally fair. Here's what I suggest: I'll send you an overview. Read it when you have time. If it resonates, hit reply and we can circle back in a month. Sound good?"

---

### MINUTES 28–30: CLOSE (Clear, Direct)

If objections are handled and they're warming up:

> "Great. So it sounds like this could be a good fit. Let's move forward. Here's what happens next: I'm going to send you a contract outlining exactly what we do, timeline, and investment. You review it, ask any questions, and once you sign, we schedule our kickoff call for [date]. Sound good?"

If still uncertain:

> "I know you want to think about it, which makes sense. Here's what I'll do: I'll send you a one-page overview of our process. You review it, and let's touch base [day/date] so you can ask any final questions. Deal?"

If the answer is no:

> "I appreciate you taking the time. I don't think this is the right fit right now, which is honest. But [relevant referral/future option]. I'm rooting for you either way."

## Before the Call: Set Yourself Up to Win

### Use a Pre-Call Questionnaire

Before anyone gets on your calendar, have them fill out a short intake form:

- What's your biggest challenge right now?
- What have you already tried?
- What would success look like in 90 days?
- What's your budget range?
- Why now?

This filters out bad fits before you spend 30 minutes talking to someone who can't afford you. It also primes the prospect — they start thinking about their problem before the call.

### Research Before You Jump On

Spend 5 minutes:
- Checking their LinkedIn or website
- Reading their intake answers closely
- Noting 2–3 specific questions based on their situation

When you reference something specific ("I saw you launched a new offer in January — how's that been going?"), they immediately trust you're paying attention.

## The 4-Part Discovery Call Framework

### Part 1: Open with Context (2–3 minutes)

Set the frame before diving in. This reduces pressure for both of you.

**Example opening:**

> "Thanks for jumping on. I have about 30 minutes set aside. What I'd like to do is understand your situation first, share what we do and how it might help, and at the end we'll both know if it makes sense to move forward — or not. Sound good?"

This one sentence does three things: it sets expectations, signals you're not going to hard-close them, and makes "not moving forward" a legitimate outcome. That openness builds trust immediately.

### Part 2: Diagnose the Problem (10–12 minutes)

This is the core of the call. Ask open questions, then dig deeper.

**Situation questions:**
- "Walk me through what's happening right now with [area they mentioned]."
- "How long has this been a problem?"
- "What does a typical week look like for you?"

**Pain questions:**
- "What's the impact of that on your business? Your revenue? Your time?"
- "Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how urgent is solving this right now?"

**Implication questions:**
- "If nothing changes in the next 6 months, where does that leave you?"
- "What opportunities are you missing because of this?"

The goal is for them to articulate their own pain clearly. When they hear themselves say "I've lost three clients because of this," it's far more powerful than you telling them they have a problem.

### Part 3: Present Your Solution (5–7 minutes)

Only after they've fully expressed the problem do you bring your offer in.

Link it directly to what they said:

> "Based on what you've shared — especially [specific pain point they mentioned] — here's how we typically help people in your situation..."

Keep it brief. Cover:
- What you do
- How it works (process overview)
- What the outcome looks like
- A quick client result if you have one

Don't list every feature. Match your offer to their specific problem. If it doesn't fit, say so. Referrals and referrals from people you turned down are worth more long-term than a bad client engagement.

### Part 4: Handle Objections and Close (5–8 minutes)

Once you've shared your solution, ask:

> "Does this sound like it could be the right fit for what you're dealing with?"

Then stop talking. The silence that follows is productive — let them think.

**Common objections and how to handle them:**

**"I need to think about it."**
> "Totally fair. What specifically would help you make a decision? Is there more information I can give you now?"

Often "I need to think about it" means "I have a concern I haven't voiced." Ask what's holding them back.

**"It's more than I expected to spend."**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — is it a budget issue, or is it a confidence issue about whether it'll work? Because those are different conversations."

This separates price objections from value objections. If they're not confident in the ROI, no discount will close them.

**"I'm not sure it's the right time."**
> "What would need to be true for it to be the right time?"

This either surfaces a real blocker you can help solve, or reveals there's no urgency — in which case, don't push.

## How to Close Without Feeling Sleazy

A close isn't a pressure move. It's a clear question:

> "Based on what we've talked about, do you want to move forward?"

Or, if you want to guide to next steps:

> "I think this could be a great fit. The next step would be [X]. Want to do that?"

If yes — great. Confirm details, send the contract.

If no — ask why. Not to argue. To learn. Every "no" is data.

## Qualifying Fast: The BANT Framework

For quick lead qualification, use BANT:

- **Budget** — Do they have money to invest? Can they afford your rate?
- **Authority** — Are they the decision-maker, or do they need to check with a partner/spouse/board?
- **Need** — Is the problem real, acknowledged, and painful enough?
- **Timeline** — Are they looking to start soon, or "someday"?

A lead who has need but no budget, or budget but no real urgency, isn't a hot lead. Stop trying to convert them now and put them in a nurture sequence.

## The 3 Mistakes That Kill Discovery Calls

**1. Talking too much**
The ratio should be roughly 70% them talking, 30% you. If you're pitching more than listening, you've lost the plot.

**2. No pre-qualification**
Jumping on calls with anyone who books wastes your most valuable resource: time. Always run a qualification step first.

**3. Caving on price**
If your first instinct when they say "that's expensive" is to offer a discount, you're signaling that your price wasn't the real price. Instead, anchor to the value and let them decide.

## Key Takeaways

- A discovery call is a diagnostic conversation, not a pitch
- Pre-qualify before you get on the phone
- Ask questions that help them articulate their own pain
- Present your offer only after you've understood the problem
- Close with a clear, direct question — then stop talking
- Follow up twice, then let it go

The best solopreneurs don't close every call. They close the right calls — and those clients stick around, refer others, and make the whole business easier.
