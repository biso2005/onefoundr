# Discovery Calls: Master the Sales Conversation as a Solopreneur

A discovery call isn't a pitch. It's a conversation to find out if you're the right fit for someone — and if they're the right fit for you.

Most solopreneurs dread sales calls because they feel like manipulation. The fix isn't a better script. It's a better mindset: your job is to understand the problem, not sell the solution.

---

## What a Discovery Call Actually Is

A discovery call is a structured conversation with a potential client where you:

1. **Understand their situation** — What's actually going on?
2. **Qualify the lead** — Are they ready, willing, and able to work with you?
3. **Help them see the cost of inaction** — What happens if nothing changes?
4. **Determine fit** — Is your offer the right solution for them?

Notice what's missing: convincing, pitching, persuading. If you're doing it right, the client sells themselves.

---

## Before the Call: Set Yourself Up to Win

### Use a Pre-Call Questionnaire

Before anyone gets on your calendar, have them fill out a short intake form:

- What's your biggest challenge right now?
- What have you already tried?
- What would success look like in 90 days?
- What's your budget range?
- Why now?

This filters out bad fits before you spend 30 minutes talking to someone who can't afford you. It also primes the prospect — they start thinking about their problem before the call.

### Research Before You Jump On

Spend 5 minutes:
- Checking their LinkedIn or website
- Reading their intake answers closely
- Noting 2–3 specific questions based on their situation

When you reference something specific ("I saw you launched a new offer in January — how's that been going?"), they immediately trust you're paying attention.

## The 4-Part Discovery Call Framework

### Part 1: Open with Context (2–3 minutes)

Set the frame before diving in. This reduces pressure for both of you.

**Example opening:**

> "Thanks for jumping on. I have about 30 minutes set aside. What I'd like to do is understand your situation first, share what we do and how it might help, and at the end we'll both know if it makes sense to move forward — or not. Sound good?"

This one sentence does three things: it sets expectations, signals you're not going to hard-close them, and makes "not moving forward" a legitimate outcome. That openness builds trust immediately.

### Part 2: Diagnose the Problem (10–12 minutes)

This is the core of the call. Ask open questions, then dig deeper.

**Situation questions:**
- "Walk me through what's happening right now with [area they mentioned]."
- "How long has this been a problem?"
- "What does a typical week look like for you?"

**Pain questions:**
- "What's the impact of that on your business? Your revenue? Your time?"
- "Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how urgent is solving this right now?"

**Implication questions:**
- "If nothing changes in the next 6 months, where does that leave you?"
- "What opportunities are you missing because of this?"

The goal is for them to articulate their own pain clearly. When they hear themselves say "I've lost three clients because of this," it's far more powerful than you telling them they have a problem.

### Part 3: Present Your Solution (5–7 minutes)

Only after they've fully expressed the problem do you bring your offer in.

Link it directly to what they said:

> "Based on what you've shared — especially [specific pain point they mentioned] — here's how we typically help people in your situation..."

Keep it brief. Cover:
- What you do
- How it works (process overview)
- What the outcome looks like
- A quick client result if you have one

Don't list every feature. Match your offer to their specific problem. If it doesn't fit, say so. Referrals and referrals from people you turned down are worth more long-term than a bad client engagement.

### Part 4: Handle Objections and Close (5–8 minutes)

Once you've shared your solution, ask:

> "Does this sound like it could be the right fit for what you're dealing with?"

Then stop talking. The silence that follows is productive — let them think.

**Common objections and how to handle them:**

**"I need to think about it."**
> "Totally fair. What specifically would help you make a decision? Is there more information I can give you now?"

Often "I need to think about it" means "I have a concern I haven't voiced." Ask what's holding them back.

**"It's more than I expected to spend."**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — is it a budget issue, or is it a confidence issue about whether it'll work? Because those are different conversations."

This separates price objections from value objections. If they're not confident in the ROI, no discount will close them.

**"I'm not sure it's the right time."**
> "What would need to be true for it to be the right time?"

This either surfaces a real blocker you can help solve, or reveals there's no urgency — in which case, don't push.

## How to Close Without Feeling Sleazy

A close isn't a pressure move. It's a clear question:

> "Based on what we've talked about, do you want to move forward?"

Or, if you want to guide to next steps:

> "I think this could be a great fit. The next step would be [X]. Want to do that?"

If yes — great. Confirm details, send the contract.

If no — ask why. Not to argue. To learn. Every "no" is data.

## Qualifying Fast: The BANT Framework

For quick lead qualification, use BANT:

- **Budget** — Do they have money to invest? Can they afford your rate?
- **Authority** — Are they the decision-maker, or do they need to check with a partner/spouse/board?
- **Need** — Is the problem real, acknowledged, and painful enough?
- **Timeline** — Are they looking to start soon, or "someday"?

A lead who has need but no budget, or budget but no real urgency, isn't a hot lead. Stop trying to convert them now and put them in a nurture sequence.

## After the Call: The Follow-Up

Send a follow-up email within 2 hours. Keep it tight:

**Subject:** Next steps from our call

> Hey [Name],
>
> Thanks for the conversation today. Based on what you shared about [specific problem], I believe [your offer] can help you [specific outcome].
>
> As discussed, the next step is [X]. I've attached [contract/proposal/info]. Let me know if you have any questions.
>
> Looking forward to working together.
>
> [Your name]

If they went quiet after a call that ended positively, follow up once more 3–4 days later:

> "Hey [Name] — just checking in. Any questions I can answer before you decide?"

That's it. Two follow-ups max. Don't chase.

---

## The 3 Mistakes That Kill Discovery Calls

**1. Talking too much**
The ratio should be roughly 70% them talking, 30% you. If you're pitching more than listening, you've lost the plot.

**2. No pre-qualification**
Jumping on calls with anyone who books wastes your most valuable resource: time. Always run a qualification step first.

**3. Caving on price**
If your first instinct when they say "that's expensive" is to offer a discount, you're signaling that your price wasn't the real price. Instead, anchor to the value and let them decide.

| 14–20 min | Present solution (linked to their problem) |
| 20–27 min | Q&A, objections |
| 27–30 min | Close or agree on next step |

## Key Takeaways

- A discovery call is a diagnostic conversation, not a pitch
- Pre-qualify before you get on the phone
- Ask questions that help them articulate their own pain
- Present your offer only after you've understood the problem
- Close with a clear, direct question — then stop talking
- Follow up twice, then let it go

The best solopreneurs don't close every call. They close the right calls — and those clients stick around, refer others, and make the whole business easier.
