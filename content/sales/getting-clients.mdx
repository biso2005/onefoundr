Your first 10 clients are the hardest to get. After that, referrals pick up, your confidence grows, and momentum builds.

But that first client? That feels impossible.

This guide walks through 5 battle-tested strategies to get your first 10 clients. You don't need a fancy website, a marketing budget, or a sales team. You need persistence and the right approach.

## Why Your First 10 Clients Matter

They're not just revenue. They're:
- **Proof points**: You can now say "I've worked with clients" (real social proof)
- **Case studies**: You can share results (with permission)
- **Confidence**: You've closed deals. You know you can do it again
- **Referrals**: Most solopreneurs get their next 10-20 clients from the first 10

## Strategy 1: Reach Out to Your Existing Network

You already know people. Use that.

**Who to contact:**

- Former colleagues
- Classmates or alumni  
- Friends who own businesses
- Family friends who run companies
- LinkedIn connections you've actually talked to

**How to reach out:**

Don't pitch. Ask for advice first.

"Hi [Name], I'm starting my freelance [service] business and I'm reaching out to people I respect. Would you have 15 minutes for a quick call? I'd love your advice on [specific thing]."

Most people will say yes. On the call:

1. Ask them about their business (listen more than talk)
2. Ask about their biggest challenges
3. If your service solves their problem, casually mention it: "That sounds like exactly what I help with"
4. Ask if they'd like to try your service

Don't force it. Many will say no, and that's fine. Some will say yes.

**Expected conversion:** 1-2 out of 10 conversations result in a client, usually at a discounted rate.

## Strategy 2: Cold Outreach to Ideal Clients

Find companies or solopreneurs who need your service and reach out via email or LinkedIn.

**How to find them:**

- Google "[your service] + [niche]" (e.g., "bookkeeping + ecommerce")
- LinkedIn search for people with job titles that match (e.g., search "founder" and filter by "ecommerce")
- Facebook groups for your target audience
- Local business directories

**How to reach out:**

Your message should:
1. Be personalized (mention something specific about them)
2. Show you understand their problem
3. Be short (3-4 sentences max)
4. Ask for a conversation, not a sale

**Example (for a copywriter):**

"Hi [Name], I noticed you just launched your email marketing course. Getting people to sign up is tough — most courses get <5% signup rates. I've helped 3 other course creators get 15%+ signup rates by rewriting their sales pages. Would it be worth a quick call to see if there's an opportunity here? —[Your name]"

**Expected conversion:** 2-5% reply rate, 10-20% of replies become clients, usually your full rate.

## Strategy 3: Referral Requests

Ask past clients or people who know you well to refer you.

Sometimes a simple email works:

"Hi [Name],

I'm now taking on freelance work as a [service]. I've worked with people like you and know I can deliver great results.

If you know anyone who needs [service], please send them my way. I'll take great care of them.

Thanks,
[Your name]"

Even better: Make it specific and offer a referral bonus.

"I'm now offering [service] to [specific type of business] who want to [specific outcome]. If you refer someone I end up working with, I'll give you $500 in credit toward future work."

**Expected conversion:** Referrals have the highest close rate (30-50%) because they come pre-warmed.

## Strategy 4: Content & Inbound Marketing

If you have time, start a blog or social presence and attract clients inbound.

Write about:
- Common problems you solve
- Tips in your niche
- Results you've gotten for clients

This takes 3-6 months to generate real leads, but the clients who come this way are highly qualified.

**Expected conversion:** Small volume initially, but high-quality leads.

## Strategy 5: Group Classes / Workshops

Offer a free or low-cost group workshop related to your service. This builds credibility and gives you a captive audience to pitch to.

Example: A bookkeeper could offer "Free Bookkeeping Basics Webinar for E-Commerce Owners." After the webinar, offer 1-on-1 bookkeeping services.

**Expected conversion:** 5-10% of attendees become clients.

## The Discovery Call

Once someone is interested, you get a discovery call. This is your chance to:

1. **Understand their problem** (Listen 70%, talk 30%)
   - "Tell me about the challenge you're facing"
   - "How long has this been an issue?"
   - "What have you tried already?"

2. **Identify if you can help** (You're qualifying them, not begging)
   - Do they have the problem you solve? If not, say so.
   - Is budget realistic? If not, move on.
   - Are they serious or just exploring? If just exploring, schedule a follow-up when they're ready.

3. **Present your solution** (If they qualify)
   - Summarize what you heard: "So the issue is X, Y, Z"
   - Show how your service solves it: "Here's how I typically approach this"
   - Give price and timeline: "This would be $X, delivered in Y days"

4. **Close or follow up**
   - "Does this feel like a fit?"
   - If yes: "Let's schedule your start date"
   - If no: "What would make this a better fit?" or "Let's stay in touch. [Follow up in 3 months]"

## Handling Objections

**"I need to think about it"**
Response: "Of course. When should we touch base? [Schedule specific follow-up date]"

**"It's too expensive"**
Response: "I understand. What would the investment need to be for you to move forward? [If they say way lower, say] That price point doesn't work for me, but I know some junior freelancers who might help. I'm happy to send referrals."

**"Can you do a discount?"**
Response: "I could reduce scope instead. Rather than [full service], we could start with [smaller service] for $X. Does that work?"

## Timeline to 10 Clients

- **Month 1**: Reach out to existing network (10-15 conversations), land 1-2 clients
- **Month 2**: More cold outreach + referral requests, land 2-3 clients
- **Month 3**: Momentum builds, landing 2-3 more clients (now at 5-8)
- **Month 4-5**: Hit 10 clients

This assumes you're reaching out to 20-30 people per month. If you only reach out to 5 people, it takes longer.

> **Hard truth:** Sales is a numbers game. The more people you reach out to, the more clients you get. Most solopreneurs fail because they reach out to 3 people, get 2 "no thanks," and give up.

Reach out to 50 people and you'll definitely get 10 clients.

## Final Principle

Your first clients won't be perfect. They might:
- Ask for discounts
- Be more demanding
- Need more hand-holding

That's fine. You want experience, testimonials, and Case studies. Once you have 10 clients and results to show, selling becomes much easier.
