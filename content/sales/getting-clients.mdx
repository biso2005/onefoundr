# Getting Your First 10 Clients: The Honest Playbook (No Theory, Just Numbers)

**The brutal truth:**

Your website doesn't get you first clients. Your social media doesn't. Your content doesn't. Your personal brand doesn't.

Your first 10 clients come from one activity: **directly asking people if they want to buy from you.**

That's it. Email. Phone. LinkedIn DM. "Hey, I do X, interested?" If yes → first client. If no → next person.

I know this because I watched it happen in reverse. A copywriter spent $4,000 on a Webflow website. 3 months, zero clients. She had a portfolio, testimonials (borrowed from her agency job), professional photos. Everything was perfect.

Then she sent 20 cold emails in one week. Within two weeks: one reply. One discovery call. One signed contract for $3,000.

But here's what changed her success rate even more: she repositioned her service into a [productized offering](/offer/productized-services) with clear boundaries, which made it much easier to close clients in those discovery calls.

That's how it works. Not strategy. Not brand positioning. Just asking.

The problem is that "just ask people" sounds unsexy. It's not a system. It's not scalable. It feels wrong, like you're bothering people.

So founders optimize for everything else instead. Pretty website. Viral TikTok strategy. Content calendar. Podcast. Personal brand.

And they get zero clients.

Let me be direct: if you spend 100 hours on things that don't generate clients, and 5 hours on direct outreach, you'll get zero clients. You can't optimize your way out of not asking.

## Here's What Actually Works (The Data)

I'm going to give you real numbers. Not "industry benchmarks." Real numbers from solo founders who went from 0 to 10 clients.

**The Channels (ranked by conversion rate for first clients):**

1. **Referrals from past work:** 15-25% conversion
2. **Cold email to ideal prospects:** 2-5% conversion
3. **LinkedIn outreach (warm-ish):** 3-8% conversion
4. **Direct asks in your network:** 10-20% conversion
5. **Warm introductions:** 20-40% conversion
6. **[Content/SEO marketing](/marketing/content-marketing):** 0% (takes 6+ months to generate any traffic)
7. **Paid ads:** 1-3% conversion (expensive to test, not worth it for first clients)
8. **Social media organic:** 0-1% conversion for first clients

**The action items look like this:**

- **Month 1:** Send 20 cold emails. Get 1-2 replies. Have 1 conversation.
- **Month 2:** Send 20 more cold emails + 5 referral asks. Get 2-3 conversations. Land 1 client (maybe).
- **Month 3:** Send 20 cold emails + 5 referral asks. Land 1-2 clients. Now you have proof.
- **Month 4-6:** Split time between new outreach (still 20-30/month) and referral requests on your first 1-2 clients. Land 2-4 more clients.
- **Month 6-12:** Mostly referrals. Send fewer cold emails. Land 5-8 more clients.

**Real timeline to 10 clients:** 8-14 months of consistent weekly effort.

Most people say "I've been trying for a year with no results." What they actually mean: "I sent 5 emails in January, made 2 posts on LinkedIn in March, and waited for inbound the rest of the year."

## The System: How to Actually Get Your First Client (Not Another Blog Post)

This is what works. Not theory. Tested with multiple founders targeting different services.

### Step 1: You Don't Need a Perfect Pitch

You need a sentence.

Not: "I help businesses optimize their marketing strategies through a comprehensive multi-channel approach."

Just: "I write landing page copy that converts. The last campaign I worked on improved signup rates from 2% to 8%."

Who is this for? **Copy three sentences like this:**

- Specific problem you solve (not your service name)
- Specific result you deliver (actual number, not vague)
- Specific type of client (not everyone)

**Examples:**
- "I help SaaS founders improve their onboarding flow. One client reduced churn from 8% to 5% in 60 days."
- "I build productized service funnels. A freelancer I worked with went from $4K to $15K/month."
- "I set up email automations that generate revenue while you sleep. Clients typically see 25-40% of revenue from automation."

That's your pitch for the next 6 months. You don't need more.

### Step 2: Find People Who Fit (Not Everyone)

Create a spreadsheet with 50-100 names.

Not "all potential clients." Specific people:
- Founders who just raised funding (they have budget)
- Companies that just hired for [role you target] (they're growing)
- People in your network who have mentioned the problem you solve
- Competitors' customers (check their testimonials, find them on LinkedIn)

**Where to find them:**
- LinkedIn (search by job title, keyword, location)
- Crunchbase (companies that raised funding in last 3 months)
- Product Hunt (recent launches in your category)
- Twitter/X (follow relevant hashtags, find people talking about your problem)
- Industry directories (Capterra, AppSumo, G2)

Add columns: Name | Company | Email | LinkedIn | Phone | How Found | Notes | Date Contacted | Response

Track everything. You'll see patterns in who responds.

### Step 3: Send Your First Email (When You're Not Ready)

This is the mistake: waiting to be ready.

You're never ready. Send it anyway.

**Email 1 (Cold, but specific):**

```
Subject: [Company name] + [specific observation]

Hi [Name],

I noticed [Company X] just launched [product/feature]. I write landing page copy for SaaS companies.

The last copy I wrote improved conversion rates from 2% → 8%.

Worth a 15-minute call to see if it makes sense?

[Your name]
```

**That's it.** 3 sentences. One goal: a conversation.

Send 5-10 of these per week. Not all Monday. Spread them across the week (Tuesday-Thursday get better response rates).

**Email 2 (If no reply after 5 days):**

```
Subject: Re: [Company name] + [specific observation]

Hi [Name],

Wanted to loop back. No pressure if you're not hiring or looking for help right now.

If you ever need [specific problem solved], let me know.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Email 3 (If still no reply after 10 days):**

One more follow-up. Then move on. Don't chase people who aren't responding. They're either not interested or not ready.

### Step 4: When They Reply (The Real Sale)

Maybe 1 in 20 emails gets a reply.

When someone replies, the sale is not the email. It's the conversation.

Call them. Video call if possible. Phone if not.

Your job in that call: **Don't pitch.**

Listen 70%. Talk 30%.

Their problems → your fit → their budget → next steps.

**The script:**
- "Thanks for getting on the call. Before I talk about what I do, tell me about [their problem]."
- Listen. Take notes. Ask clarifying questions.
- "Here's what I'm hearing: [summarize their problem]. Is that right?"
- "I've worked with [similar company] on this exact thing. We [specific result]. Would an approach like that interest you?"
- "My offer would be [specific service, specific price, specific timeline]. Does that make sense?"

If they say yes → great, you have your first client.

If they say no → "What would need to be true for this to make sense?" (Listen to their answer. They're telling you how to sell to the next person.)

### Step 5: The Money Conversation

Here's what I see: founders charge too little out of fear.

Charge too little and you:
- Attract price shoppers (worst clients)
- Set expectations of low value
- Can't afford to deliver great work
- Have to work 10x harder for same revenue

**Charge what that client is worth to you.**

For your first client, that's not your ideal rate. But it's not free, either.

**Pricing framework:**

- You want $150/hour eventually? Charge $100-120 for first clients.
- You want $5K projects eventually? Charge $2K-3K for first clients.
- You want $500/month retainers? Charge $300-400 for first clients.

Why lower? You need:
1. Proof that you deliver
2. Case studies to show the next person
3. Testimonials
4. Confidence

After 5 clients, raise your prices 25%. After 10 clients, closer to your ideal rate.

**What kills solopreneurs:** Thinking "I'll charge low to build a portfolio, then raise later." You stay at low prices because rais prices feels awkward. Stay below your bottom line.

## The Biggest Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Thinking Your Website Matters

It doesn't. Zero of your first 10 clients will come from your website.

A prospect doesn't go: "I need a copywriter... let me Google 'copywriter landing page' and hope I land on the right website."

No. A prospect says yes because:
- You emailed them specifically
- Someone referred you
- You called them
- They found you on LinkedIn

Your website is nice-to-have. It's a credibility check. But it doesn't drive clients.

Stop spending time there. Spend it on outreach.

### Mistake 2: Being Too General

"I help small businesses" = nobody wants to work with you.

"I help home service companies (plumbers, HVAC, electricians) set up appointment booking funnels that reduce no-shows" = specific, believable, sellable.

Generalists don't sell. Specialists do.

Pick one type of client. Get 5 of them. Then you can say "I specialize in working with [type]" and own it.

### Mistake 3: Long Email Lists with No Follow-Up

You send 100 emails in January.

Get 3-5 replies.

Only pitch when they ask.

But here's what most founders do: They send the email and assume one-shot is enough.

Good prospects need 3-4 touches before they're ready:

1. Initial email
2. Wait 5 days
3. One follow-up ("looping back")
4. Wait another week
5. Final "let me know if this changes"
6. Move on

I've had prospects reply after 4 weeks: "Actually, we DO need this now."

### Mistake 4: Not Asking For Referrals

Your first happy client is your best marketing asset.

After the project is done, say this:

"I loved working with you. You know the quality of my work. Is there anyone in your network who could benefit from [service]?"

50-75% of happy clients will refer you if you ask directly.

Don't be shy. They want to help.

### Mistake 5: Waiting For Perfect Before Starting

You don't have a website? Send emails anyway.

You don't have case studies yet? Still send emails. (Say: "I'm helping my first few clients, happy to discuss...")

You don't have testimonials? Cold email anyway.

Perfect is the enemy of started. Start messy. Get clients. THEN optimize.

## The Weekly Routine That Works

**5 hours per week, every single week** for your first 6 months:

- **Monday:** Review last week's metrics. Plan this week's outreach. (30 min)
- **Tuesday-Wednesday:** Send 5-10 cold emails + DMs. (1 hour)
- **Thursday:** Research and add 20 new contacts to list. (1 hour)
- **Friday:** Follow-up emails on old outreach. Follow-up calls on people who replied. (1.5 hours)
- **Whenever they reply:** Schedule call within 24 hours. (whenever)

That's it.

Everything else — your website, your content, your social media — comes after you have 5 clients.

**One more number:** Most founders get their first client within 4-6 weeks if they do this. Not 6 months. Not a year. 4-6 weeks of consistent, direct outreach.

The founders who say "I've been struggling for a year" didn't do consistent outreach. They did sporadic outreach mixed with a lot of waiting.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Direct outreach beats everything else.** Website, content, social media, paid ads — none of it compares to: "Hey, I do X, interested?"

2. **You're not ready. Do it anyway.** Send the email before your website is perfect. Before your LinkedIn looks pro. Before you have case studies. You get ready by getting clients, not the other way around.

3. **Consistency matters more than cleverness.** 5 emails every single week beats 100 emails once.

4. **Specificity converts. Generality kills.** "I help SaaS companies" beats "I help businesses." Always.

5. **Referrals are your endgame. Cash in on them.** After you deliver great work, ask for intros. This is how you hit 10+ clients.

6. **Follow up 3-4 times.** One email is not enough. Most people need multiple touches.

7. **The timeline is real: 8-14 months to 10 clients with weekly consistent effort.** Not 3 months if you're genius. Not a year if you're unlucky. Consistent effort = predictable timeline.

Your first 10 clients won't come from your brand. They'll come from you asking. That feels uncomfortable. Good. Comfortable doesn't work.
