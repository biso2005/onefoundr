# Selling Without Being Salesy: The Soft Close Framework

Most solopreneurs hate selling. They think selling means being pushy, manipulative, or sleazy.

So they don't sell at all. They hope people buy. They bury their offer so deep it feels natural. They lose money.

Selling isn't manipulation. It's helping someone solve their problem.

But you have to ask for the sale.

---

## The Selling Mindset Shift

**Old mindset**: "I hope they buy"
**New mindset**: "I'm helping them solve a problem"

When you shift your mindset:
- You're not pushy, you're helpful
- You're not salesy, you're serving
- You're not manipulative, you're clear
- You're not desperate, you're confident

The best sales pitch is telling the truth about what you offer and who it's for.

---

## The 5 Ways People Make Buying Decisions

People buy when:

### 1. They Feel Understood
"This person gets my problem"

Words that create understanding:
- "Most [type of person] struggle with X"
- "I know what it feels like to..."
- "The biggest mistake I see is..."
- "People often think X, but really Y"

### 2. They See Social Proof
"Others like me have bought and loved it"

Social proof:
- Testimonials (real quotes from real people)
- Case studies (before/after specific results)
- Numbers (1,000+ customers, 4.9 stars)
- Logos (companies using you)
- Authority (expert recommendation)

### 3. They Understand the Value
"This will improve my life/business"

Value isn't features. It's outcomes.

Not: "15 video modules, templates, and community access"
Yes: "Go from 0 clients to consistent $10k/month"

Not: "Works with 50+ platforms"
Yes: "Save 10 hours/week on repetitive tasks"

### 4. They Believe You Can Deliver
"This person can actually help me"

Credibility comes from:
- Your track record
- Results you've gotten for others
- Your expertise/background
- How you talk about solutions
- The way you interact with their questions

### 5. They Feel the Investment is Worth It
"The benefit is worth the cost"

Worth isn't about cheap. It's about value.

A $500 service worth $10,000 = yes.
A $100 service worth $50 = no.

## The Soft Close Framework

The softest close is the most effective. Here's how:

### Step 1: Build Credibility (First Meeting)

In your first interaction (sales call, initial email, web page):

**Show understanding**:
- "Here's what I see most [your audience] struggling with"
- Ask questions about their specific situation
- Listen more than talk

**Show expertise**:
- Share relevant case study
- Explain your approach
- Show you know what you're doing

**Set expectations**:
- "Here's how I typically work..."
- "This usually takes X weeks"
- "Results look like Y"

### Step 2: Share Your Offer

**Frame it as a solution to their problem**:
Not: "I have a $2,000 service"
Yes: "To solve the problem you mentioned, here's what I'd recommend..."

**What you do**:
- Explain your service/product
- Explain what they receive
- Explain what the process looks like

**Who it's for**:
- "This is for people who..."
- "This is NOT for..."
(Being clear about who it's not for actually increases belief it's for them)

**Expected outcome**:
- "Typical results are X"
- "Timeline is usually Y weeks"
- "You'll have Z"

### Step 3: Address Their Concern (Often Unspoken)

Before they ask, address the objection they're thinking:

**Concern: "Will it actually work for my situation?"**
Address: "I've worked with people like you before. Here's what happened..."

**Concern: "Am I paying too much?"**
Address: "Most people spend 2x this in time alone trying to DIY..."

**Concern: "Will there be surprises?"**
Address: "Here's exactly what's included and what's not..."

**Concern: "What if I don't see results?"**
Address: "I offer [guarantee]. We're in this together."

### Step 4: The Offer (Clear Decision Path)

**Make it easy to say yes**:
- "Here's how this works..."
- "Next steps: Schedule a call / Buy now / Email me"
- "Any questions before you decide?"

**Make it safe to say yes**:
- Payment plan option
- Guarantee or trial period
- Clear refund policy

**Make the decision simple**:
One option (not three)
Clear price
Clear next step

### Step 5: The Ask

This is the crucial part most solopreneurs skip.

**You must ask for the sale.**

Not aggressive. Just direct.

Examples:

"Sound good? Here's how to get started..."

"Does this solve what you were looking for? If so, let's do it."

"Want to move forward? Book a call here..."

"Ready to get started? I can have you onboarded by Thursday."

"Any questions I can answer? Otherwise, here's the link to purchase."

## Email Structure That Sells

Use this structure when selling via email:

### Subject Line
Create curiosity or promise result.

"How we got 20 qualified leads in 30 days"
"Is this the offer you're looking for?"

### Opening
Address them directly. Start with their problem or desire.

"You mentioned you're overwhelmed with admin work..."
"Most founders struggle with..."

### The Main Story
Share a relevant story or example.

"I worked with someone similar to you. Here's what was happening..."
"Here's what I found doesn't work..."

### What You Offer
Explain the solution.

"Here's what I recommend..."
"This is typically how I approach this..."

### Social Proof
Show others have benefited.

"Sarah went from X to Y"
"Similar clients typically see Z"

### Value Prop
Make the value crystal clear.

"You'll save X hours per week"
"You'll go from X to Y"

### The Ask
Direct call to action.

"Here's how to get started..."
"Let's set up a time to talk"

### Closing
Warm, confident, assumes they want this.

"Looking forward to working together"
"Excited to help you with this"

## The Sales Conversation Framework

When talking 1-on-1 (phone, video, in person):

### Opening (5 min)
Rapport. "How are you?"
Context. "Thanks for making time, here's what we'll cover..."

### Understanding (8 min)
Ask about their situation
Listen more than talk
Take notes on what matters to them

### Presenting (5 min)
Here's what I'd recommend
Here's how it works
Here's what others have experienced

### Objection Handling (3 min)
Acknowledge concern
Reframe it
Show how you've solved it before

### The Close (2 min)
"Does this feel like the right fit?"
If yes → "Great, here's how we move forward..."
If no → "No problem, here's what I'd recommend instead..."

### Follow-up (1 min)
"I'll send you the link next step"
"Any final questions?"

## The Reframe That Changes Everything

Most solopreneurs think: "It's wrong to ask for money"

Truth: "It's wrong to let people struggle when you can help"

If you can help someone:
- Get their first 10 clients
- Automate their admin
- Build their personal brand
- Scale their offer

And you don't mention your service, you're doing them a disservice.

Selling is serving. Ask for the sale.

## Soft Closing Phrases That Work

"What questions do you have for me?"
(Creates space for genuine discussion)

"Does this sound like it would help?"
(Gets their feedback)

"Would you want to move forward with this?"
(Direct question, not pushy)

"When could you get started?"
(Assumes yes, just asks timing)

"The next step is..."
(Clear path, no ambiguity)

"I have two spots open next month, want one?"
(Creates scarcity + clear offer)

## Mistakes Solopreneurs Make When Selling

### Mistake 1: Not Asking
You pitch but never explicitly ask if they want it.
Result: Confused prospect, missed sale

### Mistake 2: Asking Too Softly
"Um, if you want, maybe you could... no pressure though..."
Result: They don't take you seriously

### Mistake 3: Assuming They Know the Price
You never say the actual number.
Result: Confusion, no purchase

### Mistake 4: Selling Yourself, Not Results
"I have 15 years experience, I'm great at X"
Result: They don't see how it helps them

### Mistake 5: Over-explaining
Rambling, adding unnecessary details
Result: They're bored and confused

## Your First Sales Conversation

1. Understand their problem (ask questions)
2. Share your solution (how you help)
3. Show proof (others have benefited)
4. Make the offer (here's how it works, here's the investment)
5. Ask directly (want to move forward?)

That's it. 

Clean. Simple. Effective.

And not salesy at all.
