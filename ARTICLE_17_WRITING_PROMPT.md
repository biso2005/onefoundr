## Opening Hook (100-140 words)
You've been working on this for six months.

The landing page isn't perfect. The welcome sequence lacks polish. Your systems still have rough edges. So you keep tweaking. Another month goes by.

But here's the truth: Perfection is a lie that keeps you broke.

Customers don't care about perfection. They care about solving their problem. And they'll tell you what's missing once you launch.

The real barrier between you and revenue isn't quality. It's shipping.

This isn't permission to be sloppy. It's permission to be incomplete. And that's what matters.

## Structure Outline (5-6 main sections)

### Section 1: The Perfection Trap
- Why solopreneurs delay: Lack safety net (one bad launch = financial risk), feel responsible for quality, compare to established companies
- Cost of waiting: Every month is customers you don't have, revenue you don't earn, feedback you don't get
- The paradox: The more you delay, the more pressure mounts, the less willing you are to ship
- Real example: Someone spent 8 months building before launch; launch was fine, but could have launched in month 1

### Section 2: What "Done" Actually Means
**Done doesn't mean perfect. It means:**
- Solves the core problem (not all problems, just the main one)
- Clear enough that customers understand what they're getting
- Working well enough to deliver value
- Set up to gather feedback for v2.0

**Done means you can answer "yes" to:**
- Does it solve the primary problem customers came for?
- Can a new customer figure out how to use it?
- Will customers actually get results (even if not optimal)?
- Can you improve it based on feedback?

### Section 3: The MVP Checklist (Framework)
**Minimum Viable Product is:**
- One main offer (not 5)
- Clear problem it solves (not broad claims)
- Simple onboarding (not comprehensive training)
- Way to deliver value (not all the bells and whistles)
- Path to feedback (survey, email, conversation)

**What to cut for launch:**
- Premium features (add after customers ask)
- Advanced customization (start simple, add later)
- Fancy design (function over form; you can iterate design)
- Comprehensive documentation (1-page how-to guide is enough)

**What NOT to cut:**
- Solution to the core problem
- Clear communication of what they're getting
- Basic customer support (at least respond to emails)
- A way to gather feedback and iterate

### Section 4: Why Shipping Fast Beats Waiting
- Feedback loop: Week 1 on market teaches you more than 6 months in planning
- Iteration speed: "Perfect" takes 6+ months, decent v2 takes 4 weeks
- Confidence building: Shipping creates momentum and celebration; waiting kills motivation
- Real outcomes: Sales data beats prediction every time
- Customer respect: Customers want solutions, not promises; they'll help you improve

### Section 5: Permission & Reality Check
- Normal to feel scared before shipping
- It's okay if first version isn't great
- You'll improve via feedback, not by waiting
- Waiting doesn't make it better; ship gives you the path forward
- Best solopreneurs ship fast, listen, iterate—not the other way around

### Section 6: Three Shipping Rules
1. **Ship what solves the problem** (not what's perfect)
2. **Ship what you can explain in 1 minute** (clarity test)
3. **Ship and ask for feedback** (improvement happens after launch, not before)
