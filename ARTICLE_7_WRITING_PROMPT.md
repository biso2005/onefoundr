# Article 7 Writing Prompt: Pricing Strategy for Solopreneurs

## Meta
- **Topic:** "The Pricing Trap: Why Solopreneurs Undercharge (And How to Stop)"
- **Pillar:** Scaling Solo
- **Category:** Offer/Sales
- **Credibility Source:** Observational (personal experience pricing mistakes)
- **Opening Type:** Problem-First (most solopreneurs leave money on table)
- **Structure:** Problem → Framework → Real Examples → Action Steps
- **Tone:** Provocative/Contrarian (challenge conventional pricing wisdom)
- **Target Audience:** Solopreneurs charging hourly or fixed project fees who want to raise rates
- **Word Count Target:** 3,200–3,800 words
- **Read Time Target:** 13 min

## Core Problem Statement
Solopreneurs systematically undercharge for their work. They use hourly rates ($50–$150/hr), which anchors low. They accept project fees based on "what feels reasonable," which is usually 30–50% below market. They never raise prices because they fear losing customers.

Result: A solo web designer at $100/hr × 40 hrs/week = $4,000/month ($48k/year). A web designer charging $5K projects at 2 projects/month = $10K/month ($120k/year). Same skill. 2.5x more revenue.

## Key Insights to Cover

### 1. The Hourly Rate Trap
- Hourly rates ceiling your income at your time available
- They signal "interchangeable labor" not expertise
- Clients shop hourly on price, not value
- Even 2x hourly rate ($200/hr vs $100/hr) is still trapped in time-for-money
- Meta example: You could charge $200/hr and book 40 hours and make $8K. Or charge $5K per project and do 2/month and make $10K with less overhead

### 2. The Value-Based Pricing Framework
- Don't price on cost. Price on outcome.
- Your outcome for a web design client isn't "20 hours of design work"
- Your outcome is: "New site that generates 5 qualified leads/month × $5K average deal = $25K/year value"
- If you're creating $25K/year value, charging $5K (20% of value) is a steal
- Even charging $15K (60% of value) is still a deal for the client

### 3. Why Solopreneurs Feel Cheap Charging Real Prices
- Impostor syndrome: "Am I really worth $10K?"
- Market conditioning: Watched rest of industry price hourly, so they think it's normal
- Fear: "Clients will say no" (they won't if positioned right)
- Scarcity mindset: Afraid to turn down work by raising prices

### 4. The Positioning Connection
- Niched positioning makes value-based pricing easier (specific clients recognize the value)
- Generic "I'm a web designer" → hourly rates. "I build $50M SaaS sites" → value-based.
- Your price communicates your positioning. Low price says "generic." High price says "specialized."

### 5. Transition Strategy
- Don't flip overnight from hourly to value-based
- Transition: Hourly → Fixed project (by deliverable) → Value-based (by outcome)
- Real timeline: 3–6 months to shift your clients' perception
- Grandfathering: Tell current clients "New clients get packaged pricing; you stay on current terms until project ends"

### 6. The Psychology of Price Anchoring
- Your first quote anchors the conversation
- Quote low once, hard to raise
- Success story: Designer went from $80/hr fixed to $5K/package to $15K premium tier within 6 months by refusing low offers

## Opening Hook Options

**Option A (Contrarian Problem):**
"You're leaving money on the table every single day. And it's because of a decision you made 3 years ago when you first set your pricing."

**Option B (Stat-Based):**
"Solopreneurs typically charge 30–40% less than they should. Not because they're bad at pricing. But because they're using the wrong pricing model."

**Option C (Direct):**
"Hourly rates are a scam. Not because of dishonesty. But because they guarantee you'll always be broke if you ever get lazy (or sick, or exhausted)."

## Structure Framework

### 1. The Problem (Hook + Proof)
- Lead: Why solopreneurs underprice
- Sub-point: Hourly rates are the culprit
- Sub-point: The math of why hourly fails
- Bridge: "Here's what actually works"

### 2. The Value-Based Framework (3-8 sections)
- What is value-based pricing?
- How to calculate client value (outcome)
- 4 pricing model options (Hourly, Project, Package, Value-based)
- Compare economics of each
- When each model fits

### 3. Real Examples (2-3)
- Example 1: Web designer transition, pricing progression
- Example 2: Consultant moving from hourly to outcome-based
- Example 3: Your own pricing journey (if applicable)

### 4. Transition Playbook (Step-by-step)
- Step 1: Calculate your minimum value
- Step 2: Do 3 projects at new price to build proof
- Step 3: Update positioning + messaging to match price
- Step 4: Grandfather existing clients
- Step 5: Monitor and raise quarterly

### 5. Common Objections (Address these directly)
- "I'll lose clients if I raise prices"
- "I don't know how to calculate value"
- "What if I'm wrong about the value?"
- "Isn't this unethical?"

### 6. CTA + Next Steps
- Audit your current pricing model
- Calculate outcome value for 1 service
- Quote new price to 3 prospects (don't pitch yet)
- Track objections and patterns

## Tone & Voice Guidance
- **Provocative but not mean:** "Hourly rates are a trap" not "You're stupid for using them"
- **Specific examples:** Real numbers. Not vague percentages.
- **Permission-giving:** "It's not your fault you were taught to price hourly"
- **Confidence:** You know what you're talking about because you've lived it
- **Direct:** Short sentences. Punchy rhythm. No corporate jargon.

## Forbidden Phrases to Avoid
- "leverage" (as verb)
- "in today's world"
- "when it comes to"
- "unlock" / "unpack"
- "at the end of the day"
- "it's important to note"
- Any hyperlinked "hyper-niche" references (Article 1 exclusive)
