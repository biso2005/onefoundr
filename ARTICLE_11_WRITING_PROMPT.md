# Article 11 Writing Prompt: Service to Product Transition

## Meta
- **Topic:** "From Service to Product: When to Build Once, Sell Many Times"
- **Pillar:** Scaling Solo
- **Category:** Business Model/Growth
- **Credibility Source:** Framework-based (clear criteria for transition)
- **Opening Type:** Story-First (personal journey from service to product)
- **Structure:** Story intro → Problem → Three-Part Decision Framework → Real Examples → Transition Plan
- **Tone:** Strategic/Clear
- **Target Audience:** Solopreneurs with successful services who want to scale without hiring or burning out
- **Word Count Target:** 3,400–4,000 words
- **Read Time Target:** 14 min

## Core Problem
Services are time-for-money. Even at high rates ($5K–$10K projects), you're still bounded by your hours. The dream: Build once (product), sell many times (recurring passive revenue).

But not every service should become a product. And not every solopreneur should make the jump. There's a specific threshold/criteria for when it makes sense.

## Key Insights

### 1. The Service Vs. Product Spectrum
- Pure Service: You do the work. Client pays per project. (Consultant, designer, coach)
- Hybrid: You build a template/system once. Client customizes it. (Template marketplace, SOP packages)
- Semi-Product: You do initial setup; client maintains or uses recurring access. (Software + implementation)
- Pure Product: Built once; sold infinitely. (Software, course, template)

### 2. The Transition Timing Question
- NOT about wanting more money (that incentivizes bad timing)
- Right timing: When you have 3–5 clients asking for the same thing repeatedly
- Right timing: When you can describe the work in a system, not just "intuition"
- Wrong timing: When you're still learning / still customizing heavily each time
- Right timing: When you're bored doing custom work

### 3. The Three-Part Criteria Framework

**Part 1: Market Viability (Is there demand?)**
- Have you delivered the same service 3+ times?
- Do clients ask "Can I use this again myself?" or "Can you pre-build this?"
- Are you turning away work because you're out of time?
- Is the outcome repeatable across different clients?

**Part 2: Build Feasibility (Can you productize it?)**
- Can you document the process (if it's a template/system)?
- Can you build it once and sell it at lower price than custom?
- Does the product require significant ongoing support (= not a win)?
- Can you deliver product value without heavy customization?

**Part 3: Financial Sense (Does the math work?)**
- Custom service price: $10K per project
- Product price: $500–$2,000 one-time
- Annual volume needed: 20+ sales to beat one custom project
- Are you confident you can get 20+ annual sales?
- Timeline to payback: How long to recoup development time?

### 4. The Common Transitions
- **Consultant → Course:** Develop course ($500–$5K), sell to many
- **Designer → Template:** Design template once ($500–$5K build), sell to 100+ designers
- **Coach → Group Program:** 1-on-1 coaching → Group cohort (charge less, reach more)
- **Agency → Software:** Custom software → Productized SaaS
- **Custom development → Templates/Configs:** Pre-built workflow templates

### 5. What Usually Fails
- Bad timing: Tried to productize before service was standardized
- Wrong product type: Built a software product when a template would work
- Underestimated support: Product needs more hand-holding than expected
- Underpriced: Priced at 1/10th of custom work, so margins too thin
- Overbuilt: Built more features than are needed; spent 3x planned time

### 6. The Hybrid Approach (Often Works Better)
- Keep high-touch custom services (charge premium)
- Offer DIY product version (charge less, reach more)
- Example: Positioning consultant + DIY positioning course
- Example: Custom web design + template bundle for designers
- Reach both audiences without time conflict

## Opening Story Hook
Start with a personal anecdote about the moment you realized you were doing the same work over and over, and considered productizing it.
- What was the service?
- How many times had you done it?
- What triggered the thought "Can I build this once and sell many times?"
- What decision did you make? (And was it right?)

## Structure

### 1. The Service Ceiling (The Problem)
- Services scale linearly with time
- To double revenue, you double hours (or double billable rate)
- Both have limits
- The appealing dream: Build once, sell infinitely

### 2. Why Not Every Service Works as a Product
- Some work requires relationship/customization
- Some requires ongoing support (eats margins)
- Not everything can be standardized
- Forcing a product where service fits = unhappy customers

### 3. The Three-Part Decision Framework
Full breakdown of each part with questionnaire:
1. **Market Viability Checklist**
   - Have you delivered this 3+ times?
   - Did clients ask for repeatability?
   - Are you turning away work?
   - [5 more questions]

2. **Build Feasibility Checklist**
   - Can you explain this without "it's custom each time"?
   - What's the one-time build effort?
   - What's the ongoing support burden?
   - [5 more questions]

3. **Financial Viability Checklist**
   - Custom service price? ___
   - Product price you'd charge? ___
   - Break-even units/year? ___
   - Timeline to payback? ___
   - [5 more questions]

### 4. Real Examples (2–3)
- Example 1: Designer → Template → Case study numbers
- Example 2: Consultant → Course → Numbers
- Example 3: Coach → Group program → Numbers

### 5. The Transition Timeline (If You Decide to Go)
- Month 1: Standardize the service (document it completely)
- Month 2: Build the product (or MVP)
- Month 3: Test with 5 early customers
- Month 4: Refine + launch to list
- Month 5+: Measure, optimize, decide: double down or abandon

### 6. The Hybrid Option (You Might Not Realize It Exists)
- Keep premium custom service (for high-end clients)
- Offer DIY product version (for price-sensitive buyers)
- Both running simultaneously
- Different market segments; no conflict
- Examples + real mechanics

### 7. CTA
- Answer the three-part checklist for your current service
- If you score high on all three: Research 3 similar productized services as benchmarks
- If you score medium: Consider hybrid approach first
- If you score low: Keep refining the service; not yet time

## Tone Guidance
- **Strategic:** Help them think clearly about the decision
- **Honest:** Some services shouldn't be products; some solopreneurs shouldn't productize
- **Practical:** Real timelines, real math

## Success Criteria
- ✅ Clear criteria for when to productize (not "just do it")
- ✅ Real examples with numbers
- ✅ Acknowledges service/product hybrid option
- ✅ Honest about what fails
- ✅ Actionable decision framework
