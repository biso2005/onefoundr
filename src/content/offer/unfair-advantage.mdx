# Your Unfair Advantage: Stop Competing on Credentials and Start Leveraging Your Weird Background

I have a weird resume.

3 years in tech sales. 2 years in marketing. 1 year in product. 4 years running a startup. 6 months in psychology coursework (never finished).

By traditional standards: Jack of all trades, master of none.

No MBA. No certifications. No prestigious company on the resume.

But here's what that weird combination gives me:

I can talk to engineers (I speak their language). I can talk to marketing people (I did that work). I understand product decisions (I made them). I understand startup chaos (lived it). And I have random psychology knowledge that helps with behavior change.

Someone with just a sales background can't do what I do.

Someone with just a psych degree can't do it either.

The weird combination is the edge.

And I spent 3 years trying to hide it.

---

## The Credentialism Trap

Our industry has a massive bias: Credentials equal credibility.

You need a degree from Stanford. A certificate from that workshop. A book published. Speaking reps at conferences.

So we chase these things.

And we ignore what we actually have: Weird, unusual combinations of experience.

Meanwhile, people with weird backgrounds (you probably) are trying to sound more "credentialed."

You hide your sales background. You downplay your failed startup. You don't mention that year you spent on [random hobby].

You think: "That's not relevant. I need MBA-level credentials to be credible."

Wrong.

Your weird background is your competitive advantage.

---

## The Problem With Credentials-Based Positioning

Credentials say: "I have the standard qualification."

But credentials are easy to acquire (for others too).

Anyone can get an MBA. Thousands of people take that certification. Lots of people speak at conferences.

You're competing on a commodity.

Expertise positioning says: "I have deep knowledge in this specific area."

That's better. But it's still crowded.

**The real competitive advantage is specificity + weird combination.**

Example:

- **Generic:** "I'm a marketing coach"
- **Credentialed:** "I'm a marketing coach with an MBA from [school]"
- **Specific:** "I teach tech founders how to do sales-driven marketing (because I was an engineer who learned sales)"

The third one is interesting because it's *specific* (tech founders) + *weird combination* (engineer + sales).

Someone who went to business school and became purely a marketing person? They can't speak that language.

But you can.

---

## Mining Your Weird Background

Most people have weird backgrounds. They just haven't looked at them this way.

Here's how to find yours:

### Step 1: List your backgrounds (not credentials)

Example: "I worked in tech, then marketing, then started a company, then took a psychology course, then did freelance writing"

Don't filter. Just list it.

This is your "weird resume."

### Step 2: Find the unusual intersections

What *combinations* are unusual?

"Tech + marketing" = Normal (lots of people)
"Tech + marketing + failed startup + psychology interest" = Unusual

What audience cares about these combined skills?

- Founders trying to bridge engineering and business? YES
- People struggling with behavioral change in business? YES
- Solopreneurs who were previously in startups? YES

### Step 3: Invert it

What problem does your weird background solve that the credentialed expert can't?

**Tech person with MBA:** Understands business frameworks. Can speak business language. But probably doesn't understand product or engineering philosophy.

**You (tech + marketing + startup + psychology):** Can translate between the science (psychology) and the execution (marketing) and the product thinking (engineering). Plus you understand startup stress and chaos.

That's valuable and specific.

### Step 4: Own it

Don't hide the weird parts.

"I came from a tech background, moved into marketing, then studied psychology. That combination is my edge—I help [specific audience] by bringing all three lenses."

That's memorable. Specific. Differentiating.

## The Unusual Combinations People Have

Let me give you some examples of "weird backgrounds" that are actually EDGES:

- **Teacher + entrepreneur** = Can explain complex concepts to beginners (most entrepreneurs can't teach)
- **Healthcare + tech** = Understands both the compliance and the tech sides (rare combo)
- **Art + business** = Can think visually about strategy (most business people can't)
- **Military + startup** = Understands execution and leadership in chaos (discipline + uncertainty)
- **Academia + sales** = Can explain complex things and sell them (academics usually can't sell; salespeople usually can't explain)
- **Social work + business** = Understands human behavior + systems (most business people miss the human part)
- **Musician + tech** = Understands pattern recognition and physics in a different way (useful in certain tech niches)

Each of these is weird.

Each of these is an edge (if positioned right).

---

## The Specificity Formula

Once you've found your weird combination, you need to make it specific to a market.

Generic: "I help solopreneurs build better businesses"

This could be anyone. Competes on price and credibility alone.

Specific: "I help solopreneurs who are burned out from scaling too fast turn their business into a sustainable operation without sacrificing ambition"

This is narrower. It speaks to a specific problem. Weird background (burnout + scaling experience + psychology knowledge) is relevant.

The formula:

```
"I help [specific audience] with [specific problem] by bringing [your weird combination] to bear"
```

Example 1: "I help SaaS founders who are technical but struggle with marketing by bridging the gap between product thinking and customer messaging (I was an engineer who learned sales and marketing the hard way)"

Example 2: "I help coaches and consultants who are great at results but terrible at systems so you can scale without reinventing (I'm a systems thinker who worked with creative people and realized they think differently about process)"

Example 3: "I help executives transitioning to entrepreneurship by preparing them for the reality of uncertainty without losing the discipline they've built (I made that transition; I understand both worlds)"

Each one:
- Names a specific audience
- Names a specific problem
- Explains why your weird background matters

---

## The Credentials Thing (Still Matters a Little)

I'm not saying "forget credentials entirely."

If you have real credentials (degree, certification, years of deep experience), list them.

But don't lead with them.

Lead with specificity + weird combination.

Credentials are in the background.

**Bad:** "I'm a [degree] in [field] which qualifies me to [generic claim]"

**Good:** "I've spent 8 years helping [audience] with [problem] by applying [weird combination of skills]. I also have [credential] which validates the [specific expertise]."

Credentials support your story. They don't replace it.

## The Positioning Shift (3 Steps)

If you're currently "credentialed but generic," here's how to shift:

### Step 1: Audit Your Current Position

Go to your website/LinkedIn/bio.

Read your positioning.

Is it generic? Credential-focused? Or specific + interesting?

### Step 2: Identify Your Weird Edges

What's unusual about your background?

What combinations do you have that others don't?

Write them down.

### Step 3: Test the Positioning

Write 3 different positioning statements:

1. Current (if generic/credentialed)
2. Specific + weird combo (what you're shifting to)
3. Even more specific + weird (the "narrow niche" version)

Test the middle one. See if it resonates.

If it does, that's your new positioning.

## Why This Actually Works

Specificity + weird background = memorable, differentiating positioning.

When someone asks "what do you do?" 

"I'm a marketing coach" = Forgotten in 2 seconds
"I help burned-out solopreneurs build sustainable businesses using psychology + systems thinking from my background in tech startup chaos" = Interesting. Memorable. Specific.

Which one do people ask follow-up questions about?

The second one.

Which one do people refer others to?

The weird specific one.

Because people remember "that person who helps [specific audience] with [specific problem]" better than "that person who's a coach."
